# The Awakening — World Lore Compendium

**Tone:** GRITTY, SURVIVAL, MORALLY GRAY, POLITICAL, BODY HORROR
**Political Structure:** Walled city-state empires, isolationist, siege mentality
**Minor Factions:** Independent settlements, hunter guilds, black markets, religious orders
**World State:** 100 years post-Awakening. Walled civilizations are the new normal. The wilds belong to the mutated. Humanity survives by harvesting what hunts them.

## 1. WORLD OVERVIEW

### OVERVIEW -- The World After the Awakening
**Tone:** GRITTY, POLITICAL, SURVIVAL, BODY HORROR
**Political Structure:** Walled city-state empires, isolationist, siege mentality
**Status:** Stable decline. Walls hold. Barely.
**Continents:** Essenhall (European), Vessara (Asian), Nolruun (Primal/Wild)
**Population Trend:** Declining. Birth rates cannot offset hunter fatalities.
**Technology:** Medieval. Iron, stone, wood. No gunpowder. No magic.
**Core Conflict:** Humanity vs. the mutated wilds. Human vs. human over Spirit Cards. The powerful vs. the desperate.
**Surprise Types:** BEAST_INCURSION, WALL_BREACH, CARD_MALFUNCTION, POLITICAL_UPHEAVAL, RESOURCE_CRISIS, PLAGUE_OUTBREAK
**Encounter Types:** HUNTING_PARTY_AMBUSH, WILD_BEAST_ATTACK, BANDIT_RAID, CARD_TRADE_GONE_WRONG, WALL_INSPECTION_HORROR, SEA_BEAST_LANDING
**World Event Who:** Hunters, Wall Commanders, Emperors, The Broker, The Hollow Man
**World Event Where:** Along the walls, in the deep wilds, within throne rooms, beneath cities
**World Event Why:** To survive, to control card supply, to expand territory, to find truth about the Awakening
**World Event What:** Beast tides, wall breaches, card discoveries, political assassinations, expeditions into the unknown

### OVERVIEW -- The Awakening Calamity
**Type:** Cataclysmic event
**Date:** 100 years ago
**Known Cause:** Unknown to denizens. A meteor struck the deepest point of the empty ocean — the Point Nemo of this world.
**Actual Cause:** Extraterrestrial impact. No one witnessed it. No one knows.
**Effect:** All non-humanoid creatures across all continents began mutating within weeks. Size increase, aggression spike, anatomical distortion. Insects grew to the size of hounds. Mountain wolves became the size of carts. Roosters became cockatrices. Sea creatures became leviathans.
**Humanoid Effect:** None. Humans were entirely unaffected.
**Aftermath:** Civilization collapsed within a decade. Kingdoms fell. People fled behind fortifications. Within 30 years, the walled city-state model became the only viable survival strategy.
**Current Understanding:** People believe the creatures simply "awoke" — hence the name. Religious orders call it divine punishment. Scholars suspect something environmental but lack the tools to prove it.
**scan_depth:** 5

### OVERVIEW -- Spirit Cards
**Type:** Equipable supernatural artifact
**Source:** Dropped by slain mutated creatures
**Drop Rate:** Rare. Approximately 5-15% depending on creature strength.
**Appearance:** All Spirit Cards look identical regardless of source creature — a flat, palm-sized card with an iridescent surface that shimmers between grey and silver. No markings, no text, no creature imagery. The card's power is felt, not seen.
**Physical Properties:** Durable but not indestructible. Can be cracked by concentrated force. Feels cool to the touch. Slightly heavier than paper, lighter than stone.
**Tradeability:** Fully tradeable. Cards are currency, weaponry, and status symbols all at once.
**Storage:** Can be carried in pouches, boxes, or sleeves. Decks are common among veteran hunters.
**Cultural Weight:** Spirit Cards are the single most important resource in the post-Awakening world. They are the reason humanity still exists. They are also the reason people betray each other.

### OVERVIEW -- Spirit Card Variants
**Type:** Luck-based rarity tier (Normal/Holo/Animated Holo) + UNIQUE sovereign tier
**Variants:** Normal, Holo, Animated Holo, Animated Holo Sovereign (UNIQUE)
**Normal Card:** +0 modifier. Base power of the source creature. Most common drop. Functional, reliable, unremarkable.
**Holo Card:** +2 modifier. The card surface refracts light in a prismatic sheen, like oil on water. Roughly 10-15% of all card drops are Holo. The power boost is noticeable — a wolf strength card at Holo tier grants strength beyond what the wolf itself possessed.
**Animated Holo Card:** +5 modifier. Extremely rare, perhaps 2-3% of all drops. The card surface moves — shifting patterns, flowing light, almost alive. An Animated Holo from even a weak creature can compete with a Normal card from an apex predator.
**Animated Holo Sovereign Card:** UNIQUE. Not a random variant roll — a singular occurrence. +10 modifier. The card surface manifests faint imagery of the source creature and radiates a subtle warmth. Fewer than 20 confirmed to exist across all continents. Each Sovereign card is individually named and carries a residual will that can resist unworthy users. The granted ability transcends its creature origin, transforming into domain-level power. Sovereign cards carry extreme soul burden and cannot be equipped without prior spiritual conditioning through sustained Animated Holo exposure. See POWER SYSTEM -- Animated Holo Sovereign Cards.
**Luck Factor:** The variant roll (Normal/Holo/Animated Holo) is entirely random. A mutated sheep can drop an Animated Holo, and a legendary beast can drop a Normal. This randomness drives a gambling culture around hunting. Sovereign cards are exempt from this roll — they are singular events, not probability outcomes.
**Stacking:** The variant modifier stacks with the creature base power. So a Normal card from a legendary creature is still immensely powerful — it just is not at its theoretical maximum.
**Economic Impact:** Two creatures of the same species can yield cards worth vastly different amounts based on variant alone. Sovereign cards exist beyond economic valuation — they are traded for alliances, territory, and life debts.

### OVERVIEW -- Equipping Spirit Cards
**Type:** Soul-binding activation
**Process:** The user holds the card and wills it into their soul. The card responds by burning, cracking into glass shards, crumbling to dust, or dissolving into shadow — the visual effect varies by individual and seems tied to personality or emotional state.
**Resonance:** The card ceases to exist physically and becomes a metaphysical weight within the user being. The user can feel it — a presence, a warmth, a tension.
**Activation Time:** Instantaneous with intent. Can be done mid-combat.
**Unequipping:** The reverse process. The user wills the card out, and it materializes in their hand through the reverse of their personal animation (dust reforms, shards reassemble, shadows solidify, embers gather). This takes roughly one second.
**Swapping:** A user can unequip one card and equip another in rapid succession. Skilled hunters swap cards between fights to adapt to different threats.
**Destruction Risk:** While equipped, the card is metaphysically anchored. Concentrated attacks to the user torso or head can damage the equipped cards. A damaged card loses potency permanently. A destroyed card is gone forever.

### OVERVIEW -- The Three-Soul Limit
**Type:** Absolute biological constraint
**Rule:** No human can equip more than three Spirit Cards simultaneously.
**Enforcement:** Attempting to equip a fourth card results in nothing happening. The card simply does not respond. No pain, no rejection — just silence.
**Theoretical Basis:** Unknown. Scholars theorize the human soul has three vessels. No one has ever exceeded three. No exceptions have been found.
**Named Slots (Hunter Slang):** First Soul (often defensive/passive), Second Soul (often offensive/active), Third Soul (often utility/situational). This is tradition, not a rule — any card can go in any slot.
**Strategic Implications:** Card selection defines a hunter combat identity. A three-card loadout is a build, a commitment. Changing loadouts between hunts is common among veterans.
**Cultural Impact:** Three-card hunter is a common term for a full professional. Two-card implies either a novice or someone who lost a card. One-card is either desperate or specialized.
**scan_depth:** 4

### OVERVIEW -- The Walls and the Wilds
**Type:** Territorial division
**The Walls:** Massive stone, iron, and timber fortifications that surround every surviving city-state. Heights range from 20 to 60 meters depending on the city. Thickness from 5 to 15 meters. Constantly maintained, constantly threatened.
**The Wilds:** Everything outside the walls. Former farmland, forests, plains, mountains — all overrun by mutated creatures. No safe passage. No permanent settlements.
**The Buffer Zone:** The area immediately outside the walls, typically cleared to a distance of 200-500 meters. Patrolled regularly. Still dangerous.
**Farming:** Done inside the walls where possible. Limited space means limited food. Some cities maintain rooftop gardens, vertical farms, or underground fungus cultivation.
**Expeditions:** Organized hunting parties that venture beyond the buffer zone. Essential for food, resources, and Spirit Cards. High casualty rate.
**Wall Breaches:** The greatest fear. When a creature or swarm breaches the wall, the toll is catastrophic. Most cities have experienced at least one breach in their history.

### OVERVIEW -- Sea Travel and Isolation
**Type:** Geographical constraint
**Status:** Functionally impossible. Sea creatures mutated just as severely as land creatures. Leviathans, massive crustaceans, corrupted whales, razor-finned shoals.
**Result:** The three continents are entirely isolated from each other. No trade. No communication. No diplomacy between continents.
**Coastal Cities:** The most endangered settlements. Sea beasts can emerge from the water without warning. Coastal walls must extend into the sea or curve to form harbors.
**Knowledge Gap:** Each continent believes itself to be possibly the last bastion of humanity. No one knows if the other continents survived.
**Legends:** Sailors tell stories of lights on distant shores, but no expedition has returned from an intercontinental voyage in living memory.
**Implication:** Each continent has developed independently. Different cultures, different strategies, different relationships with Spirit Cards.
**scan_depth:** 4

### OVERVIEW -- The Hunter Profession
**Type:** Professional class
**Role:** Kill mutated creatures, harvest Spirit Cards, gather resources, protect expeditions.
**Social Status:** Simultaneously revered and pitied. Hunters are heroes who keep the walls safe. They are also people who will probably die screaming in the wilds.
**Hierarchy:** Novice (0 cards), Apprentice (1 card), Journeyman (2 cards), Full Hunter (3 cards), Veteran (survived 5+ years), Legend (survived 10+ years).
**Organization:** Most hunters belong to guilds or are retained by city-states. Freelance hunters exist but are viewed with suspicion.
**Equipment:** Standard medieval arms — swords, spears, bows, crossbows, armor. Spirit Cards are the force multiplier that makes engaging mutated creatures possible.
**Mortality Rate:** Approximately 60% of hunters die within their first three years. Those who survive tend to survive a long time.
**Economic Role:** Hunters are the primary source of Spirit Cards entering the economy. Guilds take a cut. City-states tax the rest.

### OVERVIEW -- Life Within the Walls
**Type:** Social structure
**Population Density:** High. Walled cities are cramped. Overcrowding is universal.
**Class Division:** Sharp. The wealthy live in the inner districts with stone buildings and clean water. The poor live in outer districts with timber shacks and shared wells.
**Food:** Scarce and rationed. Grain, root vegetables, limited livestock (chickens and pigs kept indoors). Hunters supplement with wild game.
**Water:** Collected from rain, drawn from wells, or piped from rivers that enter through grates in the wall. Contamination is a constant concern.
**Disease:** Common in cramped conditions. Plagues sweep through periodically. Healers are valued but limited to herbal medicine and basic surgery.
**Law Enforcement:** City guard maintains order. Corruption is rampant. Punishments are severe — exile beyond the walls is a death sentence used as deterrence.
**Entertainment:** Taverns, fighting pits, card gambling halls, storytellers, religious ceremonies. Life is hard; escapism is valued.

### OVERVIEW -- The Mutated World Bestiary
**Type:** Biological catalog
**Mutation Principle:** All non-humanoid creatures were affected. The mutation is proportional to the creature original biology — predators became more predatory, scavengers became more opportunistic, prey became more defensive.
**Size Increase:** Universal but variable. Minimum 3x original size. Maximum varies — some creatures grew 50x their original size.
**Aggression:** Near-universal increase. Territorial behavior expanded. Predators hunt more actively. Previously passive species now attack humans on sight.
**Anatomical Changes:** Beyond size, many creatures developed new features — additional limbs, hardened carapaces, venom glands, enhanced sensory organs.
**Intelligence:** Most mutated creatures remain animalistic. A few species demonstrate disturbingly tactical behavior — pack coordination, ambush planning, tool use.
**Reproduction:** Mutated creatures breed true. The mutation is hereditary. Each generation maintains the altered form.
**Spirit Card Generation:** Killing a mutated creature has a chance to produce a Spirit Card. The mechanism is not understood. The card materializes near the corpse moments after death.

## 2. CONTINENT -- ESSENHALL

### OVERVIEW -- Continent of Essenhall
**Type:** Continental region
**Geography:** Temperate to cold. Rolling plains, dense forests, mountain ranges, fjord-like coastlines. Harsh winters. Moderate summers.
**Culture Base:** European medieval — Germanic, Celtic, Nordic influences. Stone castles, iron forge traditions, feudal hierarchy, warrior ethos.
**Major Powers:** Hochveldt Sovereignty, Dunncarrock Dominion, Vessenmark Free Cities
**Population Estimate:** Largest of the three continents. Several hundred thousand within the walls.
**Climate Threat:** Winter drives creatures closer to the walls as food becomes scarce in the wilds. Autumn and winter are the most dangerous seasons.
**Unique Feature:** The Frostwall Frontier — the northern reaches where creatures grow largest and most aggressive, producing the most powerful Spirit Cards on the continent.
**Attitude Toward Cards:** Pragmatic and militaristic. Cards are tools of war and survival. Sentimental attachment to specific cards is discouraged in professional circles.
**scan_depth:** 4

### FACTION -- Hochveldt Sovereignty
**Type:** Walled city-state empire
**Leader:** Kaiser Aldricht Voss
**Location:** Essenhall, central plateau
**Structure:** Autocratic monarchy with a military council. The Kaiser holds absolute authority, advised by the Iron Ring — five generals who each command a wall sector.
**Population:** Largest walled city in Essenhall. Estimates range from 80,000 to 120,000 within triple-layered walls.
**Military:** The Hochveldt Vanguard — 3,000 professional soldiers, 500 licensed hunters. Heavily disciplined. Well-equipped.
**Card Policy:** All cards dropped within Sovereignty territory are subject to a 20% tax. The Kaiser vault holds the largest known collection of high-tier cards on the continent.
**Economy:** Iron mining, weapon forging, card taxation. Self-sufficient in basic food through vast interior farming districts.
**Relations:** Tense with Dunncarrock over border mining rights. Cold trade with Vessenmark. No trust anywhere.
**Internal Tension:** The Kaiser is aging. His daughter Drusilla is untested. Several Iron Ring generals have ambitions.
**Key Members:** Kaiser Aldricht Voss, General Garren Thale, General Maren Duskbane, Commander Ser Bronwen Haldane
**Surprise Types:** POLITICAL_UPHEAVAL, MILITARY_COUP, WALL_BREACH

### FACTION -- Dunncarrock Dominion
**Type:** Walled city-state empire
**Leader:** Thane Murtagh Krenshaw
**Location:** Essenhall, northern mountains
**Structure:** Clan-based oligarchy. The Thane rules with consent of five major clan heads. Decisions made by council vote; Thane holds tiebreaker.
**Population:** Moderate. 40,000 to 60,000. Carved into a mountainside, the city grows upward and inward rather than outward.
**Military:** The Stonebloods — 2,000 soldiers, 300 hunters. Specializing in mountain warfare and close-quarters combat.
**Card Policy:** Clans hoard cards. Each clan maintains its own vault. Inter-clan card trades are bitter negotiations.
**Economy:** Stone quarrying, copper mining, mountain goat herding (mutated, but manageable indoors). Poor food production — relies on aggressive hunting parties.
**Relations:** Hostile toward Hochveldt over mining territory. Indifferent to Vessenmark. Deeply isolationist.
**Internal Tension:** Clan rivalries are constant. Assassinations are not uncommon. The Thane maintains control through personal force and political cunning.
**Key Members:** Thane Murtagh Krenshaw, Huntress Elspeth Nairne, Clan Head Brennan Torvald, Clan Head Isolde Ashford
**Encounter Types:** MOUNTAIN_AMBUSH, CLAN_SKIRMISH, CAVE_BEAST_EMERGENCE

### FACTION -- Vessenmark Free Cities
**Type:** Alliance of three walled city-states
**Leader:** Merchant Council (rotating presidency)
**Location:** Essenhall, southern coast and river delta
**Structure:** Merchant oligarchy. Each of the three cities — Vessen, Marken, and Delft — governs independently but coordinates defense and trade through the Merchant Council.
**Population:** Combined 50,000 to 70,000. Spread across three smaller walled settlements connected by fortified river passages.
**Military:** The Free Shields — 1,500 soldiers, 200 hunters. Modest but well-funded. Mercenary supplement common.
**Card Policy:** Open market. Cards are trade goods with minimal taxation. The Vessenmark Card Exchange is the most active trading floor on the continent.
**Economy:** Trade, fishing (dangerous — river only), craft goods, card brokerage. Wealthiest per capita but most exposed to coastal threats.
**Relations:** Trade with both Hochveldt and Dunncarrock when possible. Trusts neither. Maintains neutrality as a business strategy.
**Internal Tension:** The three cities constantly compete for dominance. Marken wants more military spending. Delft wants more trade. Vessen wants control of the council.
**Key Members:** Corvan Meissener (Vessen), Helena Broadmarsh (Marken), Pieter Aldomore (Delft)
**Surprise Types:** TRADE_EMBARGO, SEA_BEAST_INCURSION, ECONOMIC_CRASH

### LOCATION -- Hochveldt City
**Type:** Walled city-state capital
**Location:** Central Essenhall plateau
**Geography:** Built on a raised plateau with natural cliff faces on three sides. Walls enclose approximately 4 square kilometers.
**Architecture:** Stone and iron. Austere. Function over form. Barracks, forges, granaries dominate the skyline. The Kaiser Keep sits at the highest point.
**Wall Details:** Triple walls. Outer wall: 30 meters high, 8 meters thick, iron-reinforced gates. Middle wall: 40 meters, 10 meters thick. Inner wall: 25 meters, surrounds the keep district only.
**Districts:** Outer Ring (residential, markets, forges), Middle Ring (military barracks, training grounds, hunter guild hall), Inner Ring (noble estates, Kaiser Keep, card vault).
**Notable Feature:** The Pit — an underground arena where hunters test cards in controlled combat. Also used for settling disputes.
**Threat Level:** Moderate. Plateau geography provides natural defense. Main threats come from airborne creatures and tunneling beasts.

### LOCATION -- Hochveldt City Districts
**Type:** Capital district breakdown
**Ring 1 (Inner):** Kaiser Keep at the highest point — seat of government, military command, and the primary card vault. The Aula, a great hall used for military briefings, trials, and public addresses. Noble estates line the inner wall, each walled in miniature.
**Ring 2 (Middle):** Military barracks, training grounds, and the Hunter Guild hall. The Pit — underground combat arena for card testing and dispute resolution. Forge district along the inner wall. Officer housing.
**Ring 3 (Outer):** Markets, residential blocks, forges, granaries, and the common population. Dense and noisy. Gate Street runs from the main gate to the middle wall — the primary commercial artery. Outer wall garrison stations double as emergency shelters.
**scan_depth:** 3

### LOCATION -- Dunncarrock City
**Type:** Walled mountain fortress
**Location:** Northern Essenhall mountains
**Geography:** Carved directly into a granite mountainside. The wall is partly natural cliff face, partly constructed stone barriers sealing mountain passes.
**Architecture:** Everything is stone. Tunnels, chambers, great halls carved from the mountain. Natural caves expanded into living spaces. Warm in winter, cool in summer. Perpetually dim.
**Wall Details:** Rather than a traditional wall, Dunncarrock seals three mountain passes with massive iron-bound timber gates, each 15 meters thick. The gates are reinforced daily.
**Districts:** Lower Deep (common quarters, markets, forges), Mid Hollow (clan halls, military barracks), Upper Peak (Thane hall, clan vaults, meeting chambers).
**Notable Feature:** The Echo Chamber — a vast natural cavern used for clan assemblies. Acoustics carry a whisper across 200 meters.
**Threat Level:** High. Mountain creatures are large and aggressive. Tunneling beasts are a constant threat — new tunnels are discovered weekly.

### LOCATION -- Vessenmark Cities
**Type:** Triple walled coastal settlements
**Location:** Southern Essenhall coast and river delta
**Geography:** Three cities spread across a river delta. Vessen at the coast, Marken 5 km upriver, Delft at the river fork.
**Architecture:** Timber and stone, practical but with decorative flourishes — carved eaves, painted shutters. More colorful than Hochveldt or Dunncarrock. Merchants display wealth through architecture.
**Wall Details:** Single walls per city, 20-25 meters high. Less impressive than Hochveldt triple ring but well-maintained. Delft has a secondary interior wall protecting its card exchange.
**Districts:** Dock District (Vessen only, heavily fortified against sea beasts), Market Row (in each city), Artisan Quarter, Residential Rings.
**Notable Feature:** The River Chain — a massive iron chain that can be raised between Vessen and Marken to block river traffic. Primarily defense against aquatic mutants.

### LOCATION -- Vessenmark Notable Locations
**Type:** City detail
**The Driftwood:** A dockside tavern in Vessen, popular with hunters, merchants, and off-the-books business. The owner is neutral by policy — no faction disputes inside. Information changes hands here more freely than in the Merchant Council chambers.
**Salt Street:** The medical district in Vessen, running parallel to the harbor wall. Herbalists, surgeons, and unlicensed practitioners operate side by side. Best medical care outside Hochveldt military hospitals, if you can afford it.
**Gullspit Row:** A boarding house street in the Vessen dock district. Cheap rooms, no questions asked. Transient hunters, dock workers, and people between lives. The landladies see everything and remember faces.
**The Fishmarket:** Marken's primary trade hub, built around the loading docks. Seafood, salvage, and untraceable goods. The red clay of the loading docks tracks foot traffic — a detail that matters when thefts occur.
**scan_depth:** 3

### LOCATION -- The Frostwall Frontier
**Type:** Geographic danger zone
**Location:** Northern Essenhall, beyond Dunncarrock
**Geography:** Frozen tundra, glacier fields, mountain passes. Perpetual winter. Temperatures lethal to unprepared humans within hours.
**Creature Density:** Lower numbers but individual creatures are the largest and most dangerous on the continent. Frost wyrm types, mammoth-class behemoths, ice-burrowing predators.
**Card Quality:** The best Spirit Cards on Essenhall come from Frostwall creatures. The risk-to-reward ratio attracts the most ambitious or desperate hunters.
**Expeditions:** Rare and costly. Only mounted by the wealthiest factions or most reckless hunters. Return rate: approximately 40%.
**Notable Landmark:** The Spire — a jagged ice formation visible from Dunncarrock upper peaks. Believed to be the territory of an apex predator. No expedition to the Spire has returned.

### LOCATION -- The Ashmoor Wastes
**Type:** Geographic danger zone
**Location:** Central Essenhall, between Hochveldt and Vessenmark
**Geography:** Volcanic wasteland. Ash-covered plains, geothermal vents, unstable ground. Hot, sulfurous, toxic in places.
**Creature Density:** Moderate. Creatures here are heat-resistant and territorial. Ash-crawlers (mutated centipede types), magma-backed boars, sulfurous bats.
**Card Quality:** Moderate. Some unique heat-based card abilities.
**History:** Before the Awakening, this was fertile farmland. Volcanic activity began during the first decade, possibly triggered by seismic shifts from the meteor impact.
**Notable Landmark:** The Cauldron — a massive volcanic crater that glows orange at night. Rumored to contain an ancient creature of tremendous power.

### LOCATION -- Ironveil Pass
**Type:** Critical mountain passage
**Location:** Essenhall, mountain range between Hochveldt and Dunncarrock
**Geography:** Narrow mountain pass, 3 km long, flanked by sheer cliffs. Only viable overland route between the two powers.
**Strategic Value:** Critical trade and military route. Controls the flow of goods and people between Hochveldt and Dunncarrock.
**Creature Threat:** Moderate. Mountain lions (mutated, enormous), rock-scaling insects, territorial avian predators.
**Control:** Disputed. Both Hochveldt and Dunncarrock maintain garrisons at opposite ends. Tension is constant. The pass itself is a no-man land.
**History:** Site of three major battles between the two powers in the last century. Bodies still turn up in spring thaws.
**Notable Feature:** The Iron Gate — a massive portcullis at the narrowest point, built cooperatively 80 years ago, now rusted and a source of dispute over who maintains it.

### LOCATION -- Grimwater Lake
**Type:** Inland water body
**Location:** Central-south Essenhall, within Hochveldt territory
**Geography:** Large freshwater lake, approximately 15 km across. Deep. Cold. Fed by mountain runoff.
**Creature Threat:** High. Aquatic mutations. Lake serpents, armored fish the size of boats, leech-type creatures that can drain a man in minutes.
**Economic Value:** Primary freshwater source for Hochveldt. Also a source of fish (carefully harvested from fortified platforms).
**Notable Feature:** The Stilts — a network of wooden platforms on iron pilings extending 500 meters from shore. Fishermen work from these fortified positions. Falls are fatal.
**History:** A lake serpent breached Hochveldt outer wall through the water grate 40 years ago. 200 died. The grate has since been reinforced with iron bars.

### LOCATION -- The Gullet
**Type:** Geographic danger zone
**Location:** Eastern Essenhall, between Hochveldt and the wilds
**Geography:** A massive canyon, 2 km wide, 300 meters deep, stretching for 40 km. Sheer cliff walls. A river runs at the bottom.
**Creature Density:** Extremely high. The canyon funnels creatures moving from the eastern wilds. It is a natural highway for beasts.
**Card Quality:** High volume of moderate-quality cards. Reliable hunting ground for experienced parties.
**Expeditions:** Regular. Hochveldt sends hunting parties to the canyon rim to engage creatures from above. Rappelling into the canyon is done only by the desperate or the legendary.
**Notable Feature:** The Razor Shelf — a narrow ledge halfway down the canyon wall where a hunter named Holt Errenwich once held off a pack of mutated wolves for three days. He survived. Most who try the ledge do not.

### LOCATION -- Thornvale
**Type:** Independent unwalled settlement
**Location:** Essenhall, forested region between Vessenmark and the wilds
**Geography:** Hidden in a dense forest valley. No walls. No open roads. The settlement is camouflaged and spread across tree platforms and hidden dugouts.
**Population:** Approximately 500. Exiles, outlaws, and those who choose freedom over safety.
**Survival Strategy:** Stealth and evasion rather than confrontation. Residents know the forest intimately. They avoid creatures rather than fight them.
**Card Policy:** Whatever you can carry. No governance. No taxation. No protection either.
**Reputation:** Known as a haven for criminals and renegades. Hunters sometimes visit to trade cards without oversight. The three empires officially condemn Thornvale; unofficially, they use it.
**Notable Resident:** Rydel the Untethered, a legendary outcast hunter.

## 3. CONTINENT -- VESSARA

### OVERVIEW -- Continent of Vessara
**Type:** Continental region
**Geography:** Diverse — temperate plains, bamboo forests, terraced mountains, monsoon coastlines, river deltas. Warm and humid in the south, cold and mountainous in the north.
**Culture Base:** Asian medieval — Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Indian, Southeast Asian influences. Dynastic governance, ancestor reverence, martial discipline, philosophical traditions.
**Major Powers:** Tengzhai Ascendancy, Surano Compact, Khambattar Mandate
**Population Estimate:** Second largest continent. Dense urban populations within layered walls.
**Climate Threat:** Monsoon season drives creatures from flooded lowlands toward elevated cities. The monsoon months are the bloodiest.
**Unique Feature:** The Jade Steps — a vast terraced mountain range that serves as both natural fortress and hunting ground. Creatures here are fast, agile, and cunning.
**Attitude Toward Cards:** Spiritual and strategic. Cards are viewed as extensions of the self. Bonding with a card is a philosophical commitment. Changing loadouts frequently is seen as spiritually shallow.
**scan_depth:** 4

### FACTION -- Tengzhai Ascendancy
**Type:** Walled city-state empire
**Leader:** Mandate Holder Kang Zhaoran
**Location:** Vessara, central plains and northern mountains
**Structure:** Imperial bureaucracy with divine mandate ideology. The Mandate Holder is considered heaven-chosen. Six Ministries manage governance: War, Agriculture, Works, Rites, Justice, and Cards.
**Population:** Largest on Vessara. 100,000 to 150,000 within five concentric wall rings. Dense, organized, hierarchical.
**Military:** The Ascendant Guard — 5,000 soldiers, 800 licensed hunters. Disciplined, formation-focused. Archers and spear formations optimized for wall defense.
**Card Policy:** Strictly regulated. The Ministry of Cards catalogs all known card types. Citizens must register their card loadouts. Unauthorized card use is punishable by confiscation.
**Economy:** Rice cultivation (interior paddies), silk production, ironwork, bureaucratic taxation. Most self-sufficient of the three Vessaran powers.
**Relations:** Views itself as the legitimate ruler of all Vessara. Surano and Khambattar are considered breakaway provinces. Diplomatic tension is constant.
**Internal Tension:** The Mandate Holder is aging. The six Ministries jockey for influence. The Ministry of Cards and Ministry of War have an institutional rivalry.
**Key Members:** Mandate Holder Kang Zhaoran, Minister Yeon Seoryung, Spymaster Chen Xialian, General Park Sun-ho
**Surprise Types:** BUREAUCRATIC_INTRIGUE, MANDATE_DISPUTE, WALL_BREACH, MONSOON_FLOOD

### FACTION -- Surano Compact
**Type:** Walled coastal confederation
**Leader:** Shiraishi Ren
**Location:** Vessara, eastern coast and islands
**Structure:** Compact agreement between samurai houses. The Shiraishi house holds the leadership through martial supremacy, not divine right. Four other houses each control a district.

## 5. POWER SYSTEM

### POWER SYSTEM -- Spirit Card Combat
**Type:** Combat framework
**Core Principle:** Spirit Cards transform ordinary humans into capable combatants. Without cards, a human cannot fight mutated creatures. With cards, they have a chance.
**Card Activation:** Equipped cards are always on — their effects are passive or activated by will. A strength card makes you stronger constantly. A fireball card lets you project fire when you choose.
**Cooldown Mechanism:** Cards use spiritual energy that recharges over time. Powerful abilities have longer cooldowns. A fireball might recharge in minutes. A massive strength boost might take hours.
**Energy Pool:** Each card has its own energy reserve. Using one card ability doesn't drain the others. However, maintaining three cards simultaneously creates cumulative spiritual fatigue.
**Fatigue:** Prolonged card use (days without rest) causes headaches, nosebleeds, hallucinations, eventually unconsciousness. Cards forcibly unequip when the user loses consciousness.
**Combat Synergy:** Three-card loadouts that complement each other are more effective than three random cards. A shield card + speed card + offensive card is a classic balanced build.
**Card Destruction:** Concentrated attacks can crack equipped cards. The card energy bleeds out through the crack, weakening it permanently. Complete destruction dissolves the card — it cannot be recovered.
**scan_depth:** 5

### POWER SYSTEM -- Card Ability Types
**Type:** Classification system
**Offensive Cards:** Direct damage abilities — fire projection, lightning strike, kinetic force, poison spray, sonic burst. Derived from aggressive predator creatures.
**Defensive Cards:** Protection abilities — enhanced durability, stone skin, shield projection, damage absorption, regeneration. Derived from armored or resilient creatures.
**Enhancement Cards:** Stat boosts — superhuman strength, speed, reflexes, senses, endurance. Derived from creatures that exemplify a single physical trait.
**Utility Cards:** Non-combat abilities — night vision, water breathing, thermal sensing, echolocation, plant communication, tracking. Derived from creatures with specialized survival adaptations.
**Control Cards:** Area manipulation — web creation, terrain alteration, vine growth, fog generation, ice formation. Derived from creatures that shape their environment.
**Special Cards:** Unique abilities that don't fit categories — fear projection, empathy sensing, time dilation (extremely rare), gravity manipulation (legendary only). Derived from the most unusual mutations.
**Note:** A card variant (Normal/Holo/Animated Holo) improves the ability power, range, duration, or cooldown — not its fundamental nature. A Normal fireball and an Animated Holo fireball are both fireballs. One just burns much hotter. Sovereign cards are the exception — they transcend their creature origin entirely, transforming abilities into domain-level expressions of power.

### POWER SYSTEM -- Card Destruction and Preservation
**Type:** Risk mechanics
**Destruction Trigger:** Concentrated physical or spiritual force applied directly to a card metaphysical anchor point (typically the user chest or head).
**Destruction Process:** The card cracks audibly. Light bleeds from the fracture. The ability weakens immediately. Further damage shatters the card entirely — it is gone forever.
**Partial Damage:** A cracked card loses 20-60% of its effectiveness depending on severity. Cracked cards can still be used but are unreliable. Cracked Animated Holo cards degrade to approximately Holo level.
**Preservation Strategies:** Hunters use defensive body positioning to protect equipped cards. Chest guards, reinforced helmets, and protective stances are standard. Some hunters intentionally unequip cards when taking heavy hits.
**Economic Impact:** Card destruction is permanent wealth destruction. A destroyed Animated Holo card can represent years of hunting income. This makes high-stakes combat economically devastating.
**Sovereign Degradation:** A cracked Sovereign card degrades to approximately Animated Holo level but retains its residual will — making it harder to control, not easier. A Sovereign card on the verge of destruction may resist its user or act erratically, as if fighting to survive. A destroyed Sovereign card is a world-level loss.
**Targeting:** Experienced hunters learn to target an opponent cards during human-on-human combat. A cracked card is a wounded fighter. This makes card protection a martial skill.
**Repair:** No known method exists to repair a damaged card. This is one of the most active areas of research across all continents.

### POWER SYSTEM -- Hunting Strategies
**Type:** Tactical framework
**Solo Hunting:** Extremely dangerous. Only attempted by veterans with optimized three-card loadouts. High reward — no card splitting with party members.
**Party Hunting:** Standard approach. 3-5 hunters with complementary loadouts. Roles: Tank (defensive cards), Striker (offensive cards), Scout (enhancement/utility cards), Support (healing/control cards).
**Wall Hunting:** Hunting from the wall itself. Archers and ranged card users engage creatures from safety. Limited to creatures that approach the wall. Low risk, low reward.
**Expedition Hunting:** Multi-day ventures into the deep wilds. Requires supply chains, camp fortifications, and coordination. High risk, high reward. Only mounted by organized groups.
**Bait Hunting:** Using livestock or noise to attract creatures to prepared kill zones. Effective but controversial — the bait doesn't always survive.
**Trap Hunting:** Using pit traps, deadfalls, and environmental hazards. Requires extensive preparation. Low card cost. Time-intensive. Works on stupid creatures; fails on intelligent ones.
**scan_depth:** 4

### POWER SYSTEM -- Card Synergy and the Three-Soul Doctrine
**Type:** Loadout theory
**The Doctrine:** A widely taught framework suggesting optimal loadouts follow a sword, shield, tool pattern — one offensive, one defensive, one utility card.
**Synergy Effects:** Some card combinations create emergent effects. A fire card + wind card creates firestorm. A strength card + speed card creates overwhelming force. These synergies are discovered through experimentation.
**Anti-Synergy:** Some cards conflict. A fire card + ice card can cause spiritual turbulence, reducing effectiveness of both. Experienced hunters learn which cards don't mix.
**Specialization vs. Versatility:** The eternal debate. Specialized loadouts (three offensive cards) are devastating in specific situations but vulnerable to surprises. Versatile loadouts handle anything but excel at nothing.
**Cultural Variations:** Essenhall favors versatility. Vessara favors doctrinal balance. Nolruun favors specialization based on environment.
**Legendary Loadouts:** Some hunters become famous for their specific three-card combinations. Holt Errenwich Razor, Wall, Eye build is studied across Essenhall.
**scan_depth:** 4

**Type:** Reference catalog

### POWER SYSTEM -- Card Appraisal and Valuation
**Type:** Economic framework

### POWER SYSTEM -- The Limits of Card Use
**Type:** Known constraints
**The Three-Card Limit:** Absolute. No exceptions in recorded history. No technique, training, or condition allows a fourth card.
**Spiritual Fatigue:** Extended use causes physical symptoms — nosebleeds, headaches, tremors, hallucinations. Prolonged overuse (weeks without rest) can cause permanent spiritual damage.
**Card Rejection:** Extremely rare but documented. Some individuals cannot equip certain cards. The card simply doesn't respond. No pattern has been identified. Approximately 1 in 500 people experience this.
**Age Limitation:** Children under approximately 12 years old cannot equip cards. The spiritual vessels are not yet developed. Attempts force the card back out with mild discomfort.
**Death and Cards:** When a card user dies, all equipped cards materialize beside the body within minutes. This makes battlefields rich picking grounds — and explains why some hunters kill other hunters.
**Transfer Cooldown:** After unequipping a card, there is a brief period (seconds to a minute) before a new card can be equipped. Skilled hunters train to minimize this gap.

### POWER SYSTEM -- Energy and Cooldown Mechanics
**Type:** Spiritual energy system
**Energy Reserve:** Each card contains a self-replenishing spiritual energy reserve. The reserve is not tied to the user physical stamina — it is an intrinsic property of the card.
**Recharge Rate:** Varies by card strength. Weak ability cards recharge in minutes. Moderate in tens of minutes. Powerful abilities can take hours. Legendary abilities may take a full day.
**Usage Cost:** Each activation of a card ability costs energy proportionate to the effect power. Passive abilities drain slowly. Active abilities drain in bursts.
**Depletion:** When a card energy is fully depleted, it cannot be activated until recharged. The card remains equipped but inactive. It does not unequip.
**Emergency Overuse:** In desperate situations, a user can force a depleted card to activate. This damages the card permanently. Each emergency use risks cracking or destroying the card entirely.
**Recharging:** Cards recharge fastest when the user is resting or sleeping. They recharge slowly during active combat. They do not recharge when the user is unconscious from spiritual fatigue.
**Environmental Factors:** Some environments affect recharge rates. Volcanic areas may boost heat-based cards. Underground areas may boost earth-based cards. This is theoretical and not fully documented.

### POWER SYSTEM -- Animated Holo Sovereign Cards
**Type:** Unique apex variant
**Tier Position:** Above Animated Holo. The highest known card variant in existence.
**Modifier:** +10. Doubles the Animated Holo modifier and applies it as a transformative amplification rather than a simple boost.

Names reflect the card nature: Sovereign's Reach, The Architect, Void-Suture, Crown-of-Thorns. No two Sovereign cards share a name or ability expression.

**Drop Condition:** Sovereign cards do not follow normal variant rolls. They manifest only from legendary apex predators — creatures that have survived decades, grown beyond their species norms, and developed something approaching territorial sovereignty over a region. The creature must be killed in direct face-to-face combat. No traps. No ambush from distance. No proxy kills. The kill must be earned. Even then, the drop is not guaranteed — the creature must have accumulated enough spiritual density through age and dominance to produce a Sovereign card upon death.
**Known Quantity:** Fewer than 20 Sovereign cards are confirmed to exist across all three continents. Each has been named. Each carries a reputation. Factions have gone to war over a single Sovereign card. Possession marks the wielder as a world-level individual.
**Sovereign Will:** Sovereign cards possess a residual imprint of the source creature will — not intelligence, but instinct. A Sovereign card can resist equipping by a user it deems insufficient. This resistance manifests as physical pain, spiritual recoil, and in extreme cases, the card physically refusing to dissolve during the equip attempt. The rejection causes immediate spiritual damage: nosebleeds, disorientation, and chest pain lasting hours. Multiple rejection attempts can cause permanent vessel damage.
**Sovereign Resonance:** When equipped, a Sovereign card does not merely grant enhanced ability — it transforms the ability into something fundamentally greater. A fire projection becomes a localized inferno with spatial awareness. A strength enhancement becomes gravitational authority over mass. A tracking sense becomes omniscient awareness within a domain. The ability evolves beyond its creature origin into something sovereign — domain-level power that redefines the battlefield around the user.

A conditioned user can maintain a Sovereign card for roughly 12-18 hours before requiring rest, compared to days for Animated Holo. Pushing past the limit causes accelerated symptoms: tremors, hallucinations, spiritual hemorrhaging (bleeding from eyes and ears), and eventual forced unequip. The card itself may initiate the unequip if it senses the user is in danger of soul damage — a protective instinct from the residual creature will.
**Permanent Effects:** Long-term Sovereign card users report permanent changes — heightened sensitivity to other card users, the ability to sense Sovereign cards in proximity, and subtle personality shifts aligned with the card nature. ### POWER SYSTEM -- Soul Strain and Card Conditioning
**Type:** Progressive spiritual training system
**Core Principle:** The human soul can adapt to spiritual burden through graduated exposure. Higher-tier cards exert greater strain on the soul vessels. Without conditioning, the strain causes physical and spiritual damage. With conditioning, the soul expands its capacity — but never beyond three vessels.
**Normal Cards (Zero Strain):** Any human above age 12 can equip Normal cards without preparation or risk. The soul accepts them effortlessly. No training required. No symptoms.
**Holo Cards (Minimal Strain):** First-time users may experience brief disorientation lasting seconds as the soul adjusts to increased spiritual density. Adaptation occurs within minutes. No training required. Regular Holo users develop instinctive tolerance.
**Animated Holo Cards (Significant Strain):** First equipping an Animated Holo without conditioning causes headaches, dizziness, and mild disorientation lasting 10-30 minutes. The soul vessels stretch to accommodate the denser spiritual weight. Symptoms intensify with each additional Animated Holo. With repeated exposure over weeks of regular use, the soul adapts and strain diminishes to manageable levels. Most professional hunters reach baseline Animated Holo tolerance within 3-6 months of regular use.
**Sovereign Cards (Extreme Strain):** Cannot be safely equipped without prior Animated Holo conditioning. Unconditioned users attempting to equip a Sovereign card risk immediate unconsciousness, spiritual vessel damage, or catastrophic rejection — the card forcing itself out of the soul with violent physical symptoms including vomiting, seizure-like muscle spasms, and potential coma. Even conditioned users find the first equipping an ordeal.
**Conditioning Regimen:** The standard training method is sustained Animated Holo exposure. Hunters who regularly equip and maintain Animated Holo cards gradually build spiritual endurance. The metric is tolerance — how many Animated Holos can be maintained simultaneously without symptoms.
**Conditioning Stages:**
- Stage 1 (Raw): Single Animated Holo sustainable for hours with periodic headaches. Common among new hunters.
- Stage 2 (Adapted): Multiple Animated Holos sustainable with mild symptoms — occasional nosebleeds, fatigue after extended use. Most veteran hunters.
- Stage 3 (Conditioned): Full Animated Holo loadout sustainable indefinitely with no symptoms. Rare. Marks an elite spiritual capacity.
- Stage 4 (Sovereign-Ready): The soul vessels have developed enough density to accept a Sovereign card. A lot of veteran reach this stage, the problem is the card itself, there is barely any.
**Sovereign Equipping Process:** Even at Stage 4, equipping a Sovereign card takes longer than normal. Seconds become a full minute of resistance. The user experiences intense chest pressure, heightened senses, cold sweat, and a feeling of the soul being reshaped by something vast and alien. The card may test the user during this process — a moment of spiritual confrontation where the user must assert dominance over the residual creature will. Failure means rejection. Success means the card submits and bonds.
**Sovereign Sustained Burden:** A Sovereign card is never comfortable. It is a constant presence — heavy, alive, aware. Conditioned users describe it as carrying a sleeping predator inside their chest. The weight is tolerable but never absent. Over time, users develop a relationship with the card — not communication, but mutual awareness.

### POWER SYSTEM -- The Obsidian Key
**Type:** World-class artifact (non-card)
**Nature:** Not a Spirit Card. Cannot be equipped. Cannot be crafted. Exists as a physical object — a small black key approximately 10 centimeters long, warm to the touch, faintly translucent when held to light with internal patterns that shift slowly. The material resembles obsidian but is harder than any known substance.
**Function:** An Obsidian Key can permanently link two Spirit Cards into a single soul vessel slot. The linked cards occupy one slot instead of two, effectively expanding the user capacity within the three-slot limit.
**Linking Process:** The user holds the Key between two unequipped Spirit Cards and wills the link. The Key dissolves into spiritual energy that binds both cards into a paired metaphysical entity occupying one soul vessel. The process is irreversible — the Key is consumed permanently. The two cards cannot be separated once linked.
**Maximum Capacity:** With three Obsidian Keys, a user can link three pairs of cards into three slots — six cards total. This is the theoretical maximum. No one in recorded history has possessed more than three Keys simultaneously. A user with three Keys and six cards is considered a walking catastrophe — the spiritual density of six cards sustained by one soul is immense even with the linking.
**Linked Card Properties:** Linked cards share a single soul vessel but retain their individual abilities. They can be activated independently or simultaneously. However, linked cards create a resonance feedback loop — using one card amplifies the other card presence and power within the user. This resonance creates synergistic effects: a strength card linked with a speed card produces overwhelming force that neither could achieve alone. A fire card linked with a control card produces directed intelligent flame. The linked whole is greater than the sum of its parts.
**Linked Card Risks:** The resonance feedback loop works both ways. If the linked cards have anti-synergy (fire + ice, offense + pacifist utility), the feedback causes spiritual turbulence — headaches, erratic card behavior, and potential overload during simultaneous activation. Additionally, damage to one linked card bleeds through the Key bond to the other. Destroying one card in a linked pair destroys both. This makes linked cards more vulnerable than independent cards — a single decisive hit can cost two cards at once.
**Linked Card Management:** Linked cards unequip as a pair. They cannot be split. If the user wants to change one card in the pair, the entire pair must be unequipped and a different card (or pair) equipped in that slot. The Key bond persists even when unequipped — the two cards are permanently fused.
**Obsidian Key + Sovereign Cards:** Linking a Sovereign card with another card via Obsidian Key is possible but extraordinarily dangerous. The Sovereign card residual will may conflict with or dominate the linked card. Users report that a linked Sovereign card amplifies its partner to near-Sovereign levels, but the soul strain of maintaining such a bond is nearly double that of a Sovereign card alone. Only the most spiritually conditioned individuals can sustain it.
**Known Quantity:** Fewer than 32 Obsidian Keys are confirmed to exist across all three continents. Their origin is unknown. They do not drop from creatures. They are not crafted by any known process. They are found — in pre-Awakening ruins, in deep underground vaults, in places where the world before the catastrophe left artifacts behind. Each Key discovery is a world-level event that draws the attention of every faction within travel distance.
**Rumored Locations:** The Sunken Temple (Vessara, Drowned Coast), the deepest unexplored sections of the Deep Roads (Nolruun), The Cauldron volcanic depths (Essenhall, Ashmoor Wastes), and pre-Awakening ruins reported by explorers but never confirmed or mapped.
**Political Impact:** Possession of an Obsidian Key is a strategic advantage of the highest order. Factions have launched wars over rumors of Key locations. Known Keys are held by the most powerful city-states or the most dangerous individuals alive. A hunter possessing even one Key is classified as a world-level threat by every intelligence network on their continent.
**Cultural Perception:** Obsidian Keys are viewed with a mixture of awe and fear. They break the fundamental rule of the post-Awakening world — the three-card limit. Some cultures revere them as sacred tools that restore what humanity lost. Others view them as blasphemous violations of the natural order. The Cult of the Card considers Keys the most sacred non-card objects in existence — fragments of whatever force created the Spirit Card system, designed to help humanity transcend its limits.

## 6. ECONOMY

### ECONOMY -- Card Economy and Trading
**Type:** Economic system
**Currency Status:** Spirit Cards are the closest thing to a universal currency. They have intrinsic value, are portable, durable, and universally desired.
**Coin Currency:** Traditional coinage still exists — copper, silver, gold pieces. Used for daily transactions. Cards are used for major transactions.
**Exchange Rate:** Fluid and contextual. A Holo card might be worth a house in a small settlement or a month rent in a major city.
**Card Markets:** Formal card exchanges exist in major cities. Informal trading happens in taverns, between hunters, and in places like Thornvale.
**Taxation:** All three continents tax card transactions to varying degrees. Tax evasion through private trades is rampant.
**Black Market:** Significant. Stolen cards, unregistered trades, and forced card extraction (killing for cards) are persistent criminal enterprises.
**Economic Inequality:** Card ownership is the primary wealth divide. Those with cards have power, income, and security. Those without serve those who do.
**scan_depth:** 4

### ECONOMY -- Resource Scarcity
**Type:** Economic constraint
**Food:** The scarcest resource. Walled agriculture cannot support populations. Hunting provides meat but is dangerous. Rationing is universal. Famine is a recurring threat.
**Timber:** Critical for construction, weapons, and fuel. Must be harvested outside walls. Logging expeditions are as dangerous as hunting expeditions.
**Iron:** Essential for weapons, armor, wall reinforcement, and tools. Mined where possible. Some deposits are outside walls — requiring fortified mining operations.
**Medicine:** Herbal and alchemical. Limited by available plants. Many medicinal plants grow only in the wilds. Healers are highly valued.
**Labor:** The most replaceable resource. High population density means labor is cheap. Individual life value is low in the outer districts.
**Information:** Surprisingly valuable. Maps of creature territories, card drop data, and strategic intelligence trade at premium prices.

### ECONOMY -- The Hunter Market
**Type:** Commercial ecosystem
**What Hunters Buy:** Weapons, armor, supplies, replacement cards, information, healing, companionship.
**What Hunters Sell:** Spirit Cards, creature materials (hide, bone, venom), meat, information, protection services.
**Guild Structure:** Most hunters operate through guilds that take 15-25% of earnings in exchange for equipment, information, and burial services.
**Independent Hunters:** Freelancers who keep 100% of earnings but bear 100% of costs. Higher risk, higher reward.
**Retainer System:** Wealthy nobles and merchants retain hunters as personal security. Retained hunters receive steady pay and card access in exchange for loyalty and service.
**Economic Cycle:** Hunters kill creatures, obtain cards and materials, sell to fund expeditions, kill more creatures. The cycle drives the entire post-Awakening economy.

### ECONOMY -- Wall Tax and Tribute
**Type:** Fiscal policy
**Wall Tax:** A universal tax in walled cities, levied on all residents. Funds wall maintenance, guard salaries, and emergency reserves. Paid in coin or labor.
**Card Tax:** Varies by jurisdiction. Hochveldt: 20% of cards found in territory. Tengzhai: registration requirement. Tembokarr: contribution for merit-based distribution.
**Hunting License Fees:** Hunters pay for the right to hunt outside walls. Fees fund rescue operations (nominally) and wall gate maintenance.
**Tribute:** Some smaller settlements pay tribute to larger city-states in exchange for protection. This creates client-patron relationships that mirror pre-Awakening feudalism.
**Tax Evasion:** Rampant. The informal economy is enormous. Cities estimate they collect only 40-60% of owed taxes.

### ECONOMY -- Currency and Barter
**Type:** Exchange systems
**Primary Currency:** Gold, silver, and copper coins. Minted by each city-state. Exchange rates between cities are unstable.
**Barter:** Common in outer districts and between hunters. Creature materials, food, and services trade directly.
**Card Standard:** Some merchants price high-value goods in cards rather than coin. Three Holo cards or equivalent is a recognizable price point.
**Credit:** Rare and risky. Informal credit networks exist between trusted merchants and hunters. Default is punished by exile or death.
**Inflation:** Moderate but persistent. As card supply grows (slowly), coin devalues relative to cards. Long-term trend: coins become less relevant, cards become more so.

### ECONOMY -- Inter-Faction Trade
**Type:** Economic relations
**Essenhall:** Hochveldt exports iron and weapons, imports food and timber. Dunncarrock exports stone and copper, imports food. Vessenmark exports craft goods and card brokerage services, imports raw materials.
**Vessara:** Tengzhai exports rice and bureaucratic services, imports specialty cards. Surano exports salt and fish, imports food and weapons. Khambattar exports medicine and philosophical instruction, imports building materials.
**Nolruun:** Mulaguun exports minerals, imports food and medicine. Orowai exports medicinal plants and rare creature materials, imports iron and tools. Tembokarr exports weapons, imports food and wood.
**Inter-Continental Trade:** None. Sea travel is impossible. No contact between continents.
**Trade Routes:** Dangerous overland routes connect factions within each continent. Caravans are heavily guarded. Losses to creatures and bandits are expected.

### ECONOMY -- The Cost of Living
**Type:** Daily economics
**Housing:** Inner city: expensive, stone, safe. Outer city: cheap, timber, crowded, closer to the wall (danger during breaches).
**Food:** Grain porridge is the staple. Meat is a luxury for most. Fresh vegetables are seasonal. Preserved foods are essential for winter.
**Water:** Free at public wells (long queues). Delivered water costs coin. Clean water is a health concern in dense areas.
**Card Maintenance:** Cards don't require maintenance, but protective card cases, display sleeves, and storage vaults are a cottage industry.
**Funeral Costs:** Significant. Proper burial (within walls) is expensive. Many families can only afford cremation. Pauper graves are mass pits outside the inner wall.
**Debt:** Common. Many families carry generational debt to guilds or nobles. Debt bondage is a form of slavery in all but name.

## 7. EVENTS

### ARC SUMMARY -- The First Year of the Awakening
**Type:** Historical event
**Date:** 100 years ago
**Summary:** Within weeks of the meteor impact, reports of animal attacks surged across all continents. Farmers found their dogs had doubled in size overnight. Shepherds lost entire flocks to mutated wolves. Coastal villages were swept by tide-born horrors.
**Response:** Confusion. No one understood what was happening. Local militias attempted to fight back and were slaughtered. Kingdoms deployed armies against creatures that had never been a threat.
**Turning Point:** The first Spirit Card materialized approximately six months after the Awakening began. A hunter killed a mutated wolf and found a shimmering card beside its corpse. He equipped it instinctively. He became the first card user.
**Impact:** The discovery of Spirit Cards changed everything. Within months, hunters began organizing. Within a year, the first walled settlements were being established.
**Casualties:** Estimated 40-60% of the world human population died in the first year. Entire towns consumed. Armies overrun. Civilizations collapsed.
**scan_depth:** 5

### ARC SUMMARY -- The Great Collapse
**Type:** Historical event
**Date:** 98-95 years ago
**Summary:** The period when pre-Awakening civilization fully disintegrated. Kingdoms that had survived the first year fell as food supplies dwindled, military forces were depleted, and civilian populations fled.
**Key Events:** The Fall of the Old Capitals (every continent lost its pre-Awakening capital city), the Famine Winter (food systems collapsed globally), and the First Wall Rises (survivors began fortifying existing structures).
**Social Transformation:** Feudal systems broke down. Nobility lost relevance when land became worthless. Military leaders became the new rulers. Meritocracy (of a brutal sort) replaced hereditary governance in many places.
**Cultural Loss:** Libraries burned. Knowledge was lost. Crafts and trades that depended on international supply chains disappeared. Humanity regressed technologically by centuries in some areas.
**Survivors:** Those who survived did so through geography (defensible positions), ruthlessness (abandoning the weak), or luck. The survivors became the founders of the walled city-states.
**scan_depth:** 5

### ARC SUMMARY -- The Wall Rising Era
**Type:** Historical period
**Date:** 95-70 years ago
**Summary:** The period when walled city-states were established. Survivors fortified natural defensible positions — plateaus, mountain passes, river bends. The first proper walls were crude: timber palisades, earthworks, and scavenged stone.
**Key Developments:** Standardized wall construction techniques emerged. Card-enhanced workers could build faster. Fortified agriculture (farming inside walls) was developed. The hunter profession was formalized.
**Political Solidification:** Warlords who controlled walls became rulers. Competition between walled settlements was fierce — wars over resources, territory, and Spirit Cards were constant.
**Population Stabilization:** Birth rates slowly recovered. Life inside walls was safer than the alternative. The population decline slowed and eventually stabilized.
**End of the Era:** By approximately 70 years ago, the major walled city-states had established their current borders and political structures. The world had found its new normal.
**scan_depth:** 5

### ARC SUMMARY -- The First Hunt
**Type:** Historical event
**Date:** 99 years ago
**Summary:** The story of the first Spirit Card user — a hunter whose name varies by continent (Essenhall calls him Erren, Vessara calls him Ji-Ho, Nolruun calls him Ogun). Each continent claims him as their own. He may have been three different people.
**The Event:** A hunter killed a mutated creature and found a Spirit Card. He equipped it and gained supernatural ability. He used it to defend his settlement. He became a legend.
**Significance:** The First Hunt proved that humanity could fight back. It established the fundamental relationship: kill creatures, gain cards, use cards to survive. This cycle defines the post-Awakening world.
**The Hunter Fate:** Unknown. Every version of the story ends differently. In some, he died defending his settlement. In others, he walked into the wilds and never returned. In others, his cards were stolen and he was killed by other humans.
**Cultural Impact:** The First Hunt is a foundational myth across all continents. Hunters invoke it as a blessing. Leaders cite it as justification for their authority. Scholars study it as the origin of Spirit Cards.
**scan_depth:** 5

### ARC SUMMARY -- The Leviathan Tide
**Type:** Historical event
**Date:** 60 years ago
**Summary:** The largest coordinated sea creature attack in recorded history. Creatures surged from the ocean simultaneously across all coastal settlements on all continents. No coordination — simply a mass migration triggered by unknown deep-ocean events.
**Impact:** Three coastal settlements were destroyed entirely. Dozens more were damaged. Thousands died. Coastal walls were reinforced globally in response.
**Aftermath:** Coastal cities developed the sea-wall and harbor-chain defense systems still in use today. Sea creature migration patterns became a subject of intense study. Naval ambitions were permanently abandoned.
**Legacy:** The Leviathan Tide is referenced whenever coastal defenses are discussed. It is the benchmark for worst-case scenarios. Tide-level threat is a recognized classification.
**Lessons Learned:** Sea creatures are more dangerous than land creatures of equivalent size. The ocean is not a barrier — it is a habitat. Coastal settlements will always be under threat.

### ARC SUMMARY -- The Card War of Essenhall
**Type:** Historical event
**Date:** 40 years ago
**Summary:** A brief but devastating conflict between Hochveldt and Dunncarrock over control of a newly discovered rich hunting ground in the Ashmoor region. The conflict was fought primarily over Spirit Card resources.

**Result:** Stalemate. Both sides exhausted their resources. The hunting ground was rendered unusable by the conflict — creatures were drawn to the battlefields, making the area too dangerous to harvest.
**Casualties:** Hochveldt lost approximately 400 soldiers and 30 hunters. Dunncarrock lost approximately 350 soldiers and 25 hunters. Neither side gained meaningful territory.
**Legacy:** The Ironveil Pass garrisons date from this conflict. The current tense peace is a direct result of mutual exhaustion. The buried bodies still surface during spring thaws.
**Current Relevance:** The Ashmoor has never fully recovered as a hunting ground. Creature populations have rebounded but are denser and more aggressive than before the war. The conflict solved nothing.

### ARC SUMMARY -- The Silent Season
**Type:** Recurring phenomenon
**Date:** Happens approximately every 7-10 years
**Summary:** For reasons unknown, all creatures across all continents become unusually passive for a period of 2-4 weeks. Attacks cease. The wilds go quiet. No one understands why.
**Observations:** During the Silent Season, creatures retreat to their dens and territories. They do not hunt. They do not attack walls. They barely respond to provocation.

**Type:** Historical event
**Date:** Approximately 50 years ago

## 8. RELATIONSHIPS

### RELATIONSHIP -- Continental Power Dynamics
**Type:** Political overview
**Essenhall Dynamics:** Three-way cold war. Hochveldt is the military heavyweight. Dunncarrock is the stubborn fortress. Vessenmark is the economic wildcard. None trust each other. All need each other.

**Nolruun Dynamics:** Tembokarr expansionism vs. Mulaguun isolationism vs. Orowai communalism. The continent is a pressure cooker. Tembokarr fourth wall construction is the spark that could ignite war.
**Inter-Continental:** No contact. Each continent is a closed system. If they could communicate, alliances would shift dramatically. Essenhall military + Nolruun resources + Vessara population would be a world-changing force.
**The Broker:** A figure rumored to exist on every continent. Possibly the same person. Possibly a network. Deals in information, cards, and secrets. Connects the underworld of every major city. Unconfirmed.

**Type:** Regional politics
**Hochveldt vs Dunncarrock:** Ancient territorial dispute over the Ironveil Pass mining region. The Card War of 40 years ago settled nothing. Currently in tense ceasefire. Border incidents monthly.
**Hochveldt vs Vessenmark:** Economic rivalry. Hochveldt resents Vessenmark card market dominance. Vessenmark fears Hochveldt military superiority. Trade continues because both profit.

**Internal -- Hochveldt:** The Iron Ring generals compete for influence. General Duskbane intelligence network vs General Thale military loyalty. The Kaiser plays them against each other.

### RELATIONSHIP -- Vessara Internal Politics
**Type:** Regional politics
**Tengzhai vs Surano:** Tengzhai claims Surano as a breakaway province. Surano claims independence by right of arms. Occasional border skirmishes. Full-scale war avoided only by mutual deterrence.
**Tengzhai vs Khambattar:** Tengzhai views Khambattar as a wayward spiritual province. Khambattar views Tengzhai as spiritually misguided. Diplomatic but cool. Trade exists. Trust does not.
**Surano vs Khambattar:** Best relationship on Vessara. Trade partners. Cultural exchange. Surano respects Khambattar spiritual traditions. Khambattar respects Surano martial honor. Alliance in all but name.
**Internal -- Tengzhai:** Ministry of War vs Ministry of Cards. Yeon Seoryung reformists vs conservative mandarins. The Mandate Holder health crisis looming over everything.
**Internal -- Surano:** House Shiraishi dominance challenged by younger houses. Duel culture maintains order but breeds resentment. Kuroda Mei exile remains a sore point.
**Internal -- Khambattar:** Sage of Wrath expansionists vs Sage of Mercy pacifists. The High Sage mediates but is frail. A schism would destroy the Mandate unity.

### RELATIONSHIP -- Nolruun Internal Politics
**Type:** Regional politics
**Mulaguun vs Tembokarr:** Territorial dispute over the volcanic border zone between the mountains and the Ashlands. Both claim mining rights. Tembokarr fourth wall construction encroaches on disputed territory.
**Mulaguun vs Orowai:** Friendly. Trade partners. Orowai provides medicinal plants and jungle materials. Mulaguun provides minerals and forged tools. Mutual defense agreement against Tembokarr expansion.
**Orowai vs Tembokarr:** Ideological enmity. Orowai communalism vs Tembokarr meritocratic aggression. Tembokarr views Orowai as weak. Orowai views Tembokarr as barbaric.
**Internal -- Mulaguun:** Elder families debate isolationism vs exploration. The Deep King favors cautious expansion. Some elders want to seal the Deep Roads entirely. Princess Nkechi pushes for more exploration.
**Internal -- Orowai:** Young generation questions isolationism. The Speaker maintains consensus but the debate grows. Yara Wildheart beast-taming experiments divide the community.
**Internal -- Tembokarr:** Succession positioning. Multiple champions preparing to challenge Kraal. Forge-Master Daeva controls weapon supply — a political lever. The Ash Pit awaits.

### RELATIONSHIP -- Cross-Continental Relations
**Type:** Inter-continental (theoretical)
**Status:** No contact. Zero communication between continents for 100 years.
**Knowledge Gap:** Each continent believes the others may have fallen. Maps from before the Awakening show three landmasses, but no one has verified their survival.
**Cultural Drift:** 100 years of isolation has created three distinct cultural responses to the same catastrophe. Essenhall = militaristic pragmatism. Vessara = spiritual bureaucracy. Nolruun = communal survivalism.
**Potential Contact:** The Tenuv legend suggests contact is possible. If a sea route exists, it would be the most transformative discovery since Spirit Cards.
**Theoretical Alliance:** Essenhall military tradition + Vessara population and organization + Nolruun resource diversity would create an unprecedented power bloc. Or it would create the largest war in history.

### RELATIONSHIP -- Underground Networks
**Type:** Criminal/extra-legal
**Card Smuggling:** Exists on every continent. Moving stolen or untaxed cards between cities. Punished by confiscation and exile (or execution, depending on jurisdiction).
**The Broker Network:** Rumored organization connecting underworld figures across cities and continents. If it exists, it is the only inter-continental human organization. Unconfirmed.
**Bandit Hunters:** Humans who hunt other humans for their cards. Operating along trade routes and in the wilds. Despised universally but difficult to eradicate.
**Information Brokers:** Those who trade in secrets. Creature migration patterns, card drop rates, political intelligence, military deployments. Their currency is knowledge.
**Thornvale (Essenhall):** The largest known extra-legal settlement. Sanctuary for exiles, criminals, and free agents. Unofficially tolerated by all three empires as a pressure valve.
**Forge-Master Daeva (Nolruun):** Controls Tembokarr weapon production. Not strictly criminal but operates with independence that borders on sovereignty. A power unto herself.

### RELATIONSHIP -- Named Underground Organizations
**Type:** Organized extra-legal groups
**Sovereign's Weave:** A secretive organization that tracks, monitors, and acquires Sovereign-tier cards. They believe Sovereign cards are fragments of whatever caused the Awakening. Their operatives — Curators — are embedded in cities across all continents. They rarely steal directly; instead they engineer situations where cards change hands. Led by a figure called the Overseer. Present everywhere, visible nowhere.
**Card Syndicates:** Local criminal networks in every major city specializing in card theft and fencing. Each city has its own syndicate; they share intelligence through the Broker Network. Common operations: stealing cards from wounded hunters, smuggling untaxed cards between cities, fencing through legitimate fronts. Collectively they move more cards than any legal market.
**The Broker Network:** A loose web of information brokers, fixers, and middlemen operating across all cities. Not a hierarchy — a mesh. If you need to find someone, acquire something illegal, or disappear, the Network can arrange it. The Broker individual may or may not sit at its center. The Network does not take sides; it takes payment.

## 9. RULES

### RULES -- World Physics and Limitations
**Type:** Fundamental rules
**No Magic:** There is no magic in this world. Spirit Cards are supernatural but they are not magic in the traditional sense. They are an unexplained phenomenon. No spells, no enchantments, no wizards.
**Medieval Technology:** Iron, steel, stone, wood, rope. No gunpowder. No electricity. No steam power. No printing press in common use. Knowledge is hand-copied.
**No Non-Human Intelligent Species:** Humans are the only intelligent species. No elves, dwarves, orcs, or other races. The world is human vs. nature (mutated).
**Mutation is Universal:** Every non-humanoid creature on the planet was affected by the Awakening. No exceptions have been found. Even creatures in isolated environments mutated.
**Mutation is Hereditary:** Mutated creatures breed true. The offspring are also mutated. There is no reversion. The world will never go back to normal.
**Spirit Cards are Mysterious:** No one understands why cards appear, why three is the limit, or why they work the way they do. These are facts, not explained mechanisms.
**scan_depth:** 5

### RULES -- Spirit Card Mechanics
**Type:** Gameplay rules
**Three-Card Limit:** Absolute maximum. No exceptions. No workarounds. This is the most important rule in the world.
**Card Variants:** Normal (+0), Holo (+2), Animated Holo (+5). The variant is random and independent of creature type.
**Card Drop Rate:** Varies by creature. Weak creatures: near zero. Common creatures: very rare. Large creatures: uncommon. Apex predators: moderate. Legendary: guaranteed.
**Equipping:** Instantaneous by will. Card dissolves into the user soul with a visual effect unique to the individual.
**Unequipping:** One second. Card materializes in hand through reverse visual effect.
**Destruction:** Concentrated attacks can crack or destroy cards. Permanent. No repair possible.
**Energy System:** Self-recharging. Cooldown-based. Powerful abilities take longer to recharge. Emergency overuse damages the card.
**Death Drop:** Cards materialize beside the body when the user dies. Always.
**scan_depth:** 5

### RULES -- Combat Rules
**Type:** Engagement rules
**Initiative:** Speed and awareness determine who acts first. Cards that enhance reflexes provide advantage.
**Card Activation:** Active abilities require conscious will and energy. Passive abilities are always on.
**Damage:** Physical damage is real and conventional. A sword wound bleeds regardless of cards. Cards enhance capabilities; they don't make users invulnerable.
**Card Destruction in Combat:** Possible when the user takes concentrated damage to the chest or head. Represented by targeting the card metaphysical anchor.
**Fatigue:** Extended combat with three cards active causes cumulative fatigue. Headaches, nosebleeds, tremors. Prolonged fights favor the side with better endurance management.
**Human vs Human:** Card users fighting other card users is common. Strategy involves targeting opponent cards while protecting your own. Card destruction is a valid (if dishonorable) tactic.
**Human vs Creature:** The standard engagement. Creatures have physical advantages (size, strength, natural weapons). Humans have card versatility and tactical intelligence. Teamwork is essential.
**scan_depth:** 4

### RULES -- Survival and Resource Rules
**Type:** Environmental rules
**Food and Water:** Must be secured regularly. Extended expeditions require supply chains. Dehydration and starvation are as deadly as creatures.
**Shelter:** Required outside walls. Night in the wilds without shelter is suicide. Temporary fortifications are standard for expeditions.
**Weather:** Extreme weather affects combat and movement. Winter in Essenhall, monsoons in Vessara, heat in Nolruun. Weather planning is critical.
**Creature Behavior:** Most creatures are territorial. They attack intruders. Some are ambush predators. A few demonstrate tactical intelligence. Understanding behavior is key to survival.
**Injury:** Without cards or medicine, injuries are life-threatening. Even with healing cards, serious wounds require time and rest. Infection is a constant threat.
**Darkness:** The wilds at night are death. Creatures are more active. Visibility is limited. Only hunters with night-vision cards operate effectively at night.
**scan_depth:** 4

### RULES -- Wall Defense Protocols
**Type:** Defensive rules
**Wall Maintenance:** Daily inspections. Weekly repairs. Monthly structural assessments. Neglect kills.
**Gate Security:** Triple-locked. Guarded 24/7. Open only for scheduled expeditions and emergencies. No exceptions without commander authorization.
**Signal System:** Fire signals for creature approach. Bell signals for wall breach. Horn signals for general alarm. Each city has its own code.
**Patrol Schedule:** Wall patrols every 2 hours. Buffer zone patrols daily. Extended patrols weekly. Pattern variation to prevent creature learning.
**Breach Protocol:** Seal affected section. Evacuate outer district. Deploy hunters to kill zone. Engineers assess damage. Priority: save lives, then restore wall integrity.
**Emergency Rations:** Every city maintains emergency food and water stores for siege situations. Typically 30 days of basic rations. Refreshed annually.
**scan_depth:** 4

### RULES -- Character Creation Framework
**Type:** Player setup rules
**Starting Area:** Player chooses one of three continents: Essenhall, Vessara, or Nolruun. Starting city determined by background choice.
**Background Options:** Noble, Soldier, Hunter Apprentice, Merchant, Healer, Scholar, Orphan, Farmer, Criminal, Isekai (Outsider)
**Starting Cards by Background:**
- Noble: 2 cards (family-provided, moderate quality)
- Soldier: 1 card (military-issued, defensive type)
- Hunter Apprentice: 1 card (mentor-gifted, varies)
- Merchant: 1 card (purchased, utility type)
- Healer: 1 card (healing/herbal type)
- Scholar: 0-1 cards (research-related if any)
- Orphan: 0 cards (must earn first card through survival)
- Farmer: 0 cards (lived inside walls, no hunting experience)
- Criminal: 1-2 cards (stolen, quality varies, risk of recognition)
- Isekai: 0 cards (arrived with nothing, but possesses knowledge from another world)
**Isekai Background:** The player character is from another world entirely. They appeared in this world through unknown means. They have no cards, no connections, and no knowledge of local customs — but they have outside perspective, literacy in an unknown script, and ideas that no one in this world has considered.
**Character Attributes:** Nature (connection to the wild), Training (combat skill), Emotion (emotional intelligence), Social (persuasion and politics), Belief (conviction and willpower), Ego (self-importance and ambition), Affinity (rapport with others)
**scan_depth:** 5

### RULES -- Isekai Background Rules
**Type:** Special background rules
**Arrival:** Isekai characters appear in the world with no explanation. They materialize in random locations — sometimes inside walls, sometimes in the wilds. Survival of arrival is not guaranteed.
**Knowledge Gap:** Isekai characters do not know about Spirit Cards, the Awakening, creature types, or local politics until they learn through play. They start truly ignorant.
**Outside Perspective:** Isekai characters may suggest solutions that no one in this world would consider. Basic scientific principles, organizational strategies, or technological concepts that don't exist here.
**Language:** Isekai characters can communicate but their speech patterns, idioms, and references are distinctly foreign. This marks them as outsiders.
**Prejudice:** Reactions to isekai characters vary. Some view them as cursed or touched by the Awakening. Others view them as useful oddities. A few religious traditions view outsiders as prophets.
**No Special Powers:** Isekai characters have no inherent supernatural abilities. They must acquire and use Spirit Cards like everyone else. Their advantage is knowledge, not power.
**Cover Story:** Isekai characters often fabricate backgrounds to explain their ignorance. Claiming amnesia, being from a distant settlement, or being a hermit are common covers.
**scan_depth:** 5

### RULES -- Progression and Growth
**Type:** Character development
**Card Acquisition:** The primary method of character growth. New cards come from hunting, trading, gifts, rewards, or theft.
**Skill Improvement:** Combat skills, survival skills, social skills, and knowledge all improve through practice and experience. No supernatural mechanism — just learning.
**Reputation:** Actions build reputation. Reputation affects how NPCs react. A famous hunter gets better prices, more respect, and more dangerous opportunities.
**Relationships:** Connections to factions and NPCs provide resources, information, and quest opportunities. Betrayal has consequences.
**Physical Changes:** Scars, injuries, aging. Combat leaves marks. These affect appearance and sometimes capabilities.
**Spiritual Growth:** Extended card use gradually increases the user spiritual capacity. They can maintain cards longer before fatigue. This is subtle and takes years. No one goes from 3 cards to 4.
**scan_depth:** 4

### RULES -- NPC Card Power Scaling
**Type:** GM reference guide
**Variant Rarity Reminder:** Normal cards (~83% of all drops) are what most of the world uses. Holo (~14%) is rare enough to command premium prices and mark someone as notably equipped. Animated Holo (~3%) is top-tier — possessing one marks a hunter as elite. Animated Holo Sovereign (<20 total) is legendary — ownership reshapes politics. Even an Animated Holo from a weak creature is prestigious, though it remains weaker than a Normal card from an apex predator.
**No Cards:** Most civilians. Farmers, laborers, servants, children. They survive behind walls and hope they never need power.
**1 Card, Normal:** City guards, junior hunters, street-level criminals, dock workers who've saved. The backbone of armed society. A Normal defensive card on a guard makes them useful against small creatures and intimidates the unarmed.
**1 Card, Holo:** Experienced guards, successful merchants, mid-tier criminals. Uncommon and respected. A Holo card can shift a bar fight or a street confrontation decisively.
**2 Cards, Normal:** Career hunters, military officers, veteran guards. Professional card users. Their two-card loadout is a deliberate build — typically one offensive, one defensive or utility.
**2 Cards, Mixed (1 Holo):** Elite hunters, guildmasters, senior military, wealthy nobles. The Holo card is their signature. They are significant local figures.
**2 Cards, Holo+:** Very rare. Senior hunter guild leaders, faction champions, card brokers of means. Anyone with two Holo cards is a known quantity in their region.
**3 Cards, Normal:** Veteran hunters who've survived decades. Military commanders. Career survivors. Not flashy, but the three-card synergy makes them dangerous in ways that matter.
**3 Cards, Mixed:** The hunter elite. Most famous hunters fall here. Their loadout is a personal legend — each card has a story. Recognized across their continent.
**3 Cards, Holo+:** Extraordinary. A handful per continent. The kind of hunter whose name is known on other continents through rumor. Their combat capability approaches military significance.
**Animated Holo (any slot):** Possessing even one Animated Holo puts someone in the top fraction of a percent. It is a conversation-stopper, a reputation-maker, and a target on the back. People want what they have.
**Noble Loadouts:** Typically 2-3 cards inherited or purchased rather than earned through hunting. Often enhancement or utility types — family-oriented cards that support governance, social manipulation, or personal defense rather than raw combat. Quality varies; old noble families hoard Holo variants.
**Criminal Loadouts:** Stolen or traded. Often mismatched — cards that don't synergize because they weren't chosen, they were acquired by opportunity. Desperation makes criminals dangerous; poor synergy makes them beatable.
**scan_depth:** 4

### CULTURE -- Information Flow in the Walled World
**Type:** How knowledge moves
**Taverns and Inns:** The primary information exchange. Hunters, merchants, and travelers share stories, warnings, and rumors. The Driftwood in Vessenmark, The Pit in Hochveldt, Waystation Seven on the Silk Road. Where drinks flow, information follows.
**Harbor Gossip:** In any coastal or river city, dock workers and harbor guards hear everything. Cargo manifests reveal trade secrets. Ship crews carry news between cities faster than official couriers. A customs clerk with loose lips is worth more than a spy.
**Hunter Guild Bulletins:** Posted notices in guild halls — creature sightings, migration patterns, active bounties, hunter deaths. Restricted to guild members but nothing stops a member from sharing.
**Merchant Couriers:** The fastest official communication between cities. Merchant houses run courier networks for trade intelligence. These networks can be bribed, intercepted, or infiltrated. Most faction intelligence passes through merchant hands at some point.
**Guard Reports:** City guard logs document incidents, card registrations, wall breaches, and criminal activity. Accessible to guard officers and, unofficially, to anyone willing to pay a clerk. Guard Captain Vane in Vessenmark, Clerk Obbit in Hochveldt — every city has someone who leaks for the right price.
**The Broker Network:** If the above channels fail, the Network can find any piece of information. The price scales with rarity. Common knowledge costs copper. Military deployments cost silver. A Sovereign card location costs more than most people earn in a lifetime.
**What Does NOT Travel:** Inter-continental information. Zero reliable communication between continents. Any knowledge about Essenhall in Vessara is at least decades old, filtered through rumor, and probably wrong. The Broker may be the only exception, and even that is unconfirmed.
**scan_depth:** 3

## 10. CULTURE

### CULTURE -- Essenhall Traditions
**Type:** Cultural norms
**Death Customs:** Cremation is standard. Ashes scattered from the wall — symbolic return to the wild. Hunters who die in the wilds are left where they fall. Retrieving a body is a mark of respect but not expected.
**Coming of Age:** At 14, children may attempt their first card equip. Success is celebrated with a naming ceremony where they receive their adult name. Failure simply means waiting another year.
**Marriage:** Arranged marriages among nobility. Commoners marry for partnership and survival. Marriages between hunter families are strategic — combining card resources.
**Festivals:** The Wall Day (anniversary of each city wall completion), The First Hunt Remembrance (annual hunter memorial), Winter Solstice (darkest night, largest communal fires).
**Superstitions:** Never say a creature name three times near the wall. Never unequip all three cards simultaneously in the wilds. Never touch a dead creature before checking for a card.
**Honor System:** Strength and duty are the highest virtues. Cowardice is the worst sin. Keeping your word matters more than keeping your life.
**scan_depth:** 3

### CULTURE -- Vessara Traditions
**Type:** Cultural norms
**Death Customs:** Ancestor veneration. The dead are remembered through shrine offerings. In Tengzhai, death records are maintained by the Ministry of Rites. In Surano, ancestor blades are passed down.
**Coming of Age:** Varies by region. Tengzhai: bureaucratic examination at 15. Surano: first duel at 16. Khambattar: spiritual retreat at 14, returning with a personal principle.
**Marriage:** Tengzhai: arranged by family and ministry approval. Surano: duel-based courtship (non-lethal combat to prove worthiness). Khambattar: spiritual compatibility assessment by the Sages.
**Festivals:** Monsoon Festival (preparing for the dangerous season), The Breach Memorial (Tengzhai only, remembrance of the 200 lost), Jade Steps Challenge (annual hunting competition).
**Superstitions:** Cards choose their wielders — a common belief even though evidence suggests otherwise. Unequipping a card in anger causes bad luck. Finding a card in the rain is auspicious.
**Honor System:** Harmony and discipline. The collective over the individual. Duty to family and faction. Loss of face is worse than loss of life in some traditions.
**scan_depth:** 3

### CULTURE -- Nolruun Traditions
**Type:** Cultural norms
**Death Customs:** Return to the earth. Mulaguun: burial in deep chambers. Orowai: body placed in the canopy to return to the trees. Tembokarr: body burned in the Ash Pit, a warrior funeral.
**Coming of Age:** Mulaguun: solo mapping expedition into a known tunnel. Orowai: canopy run across the full territory. Tembokarr: first Ash Pit match (non-lethal but painful).
**Marriage:** Mulaguun: arranged by elders. Orowai: community celebration, partnership-based. Tembokarr: challenge-based — must prove worthy through combat or service.
**Festivals:** The Great Silence (celebrated during Silent Season), Deep Day (Mulaguun, honoring the mountain), Canopy Bloom (Orowai, celebrating the living wall), Ash Festival (Tembokarr, forge demonstrations and combat tournaments).
**Superstitions:** The Heart Tree sees everything (Orowai). The Deep Roads remember (Mulaguun). The Ash Pit judges the worthy (Tembokarr). Never kill a creature that doesn't fight back.
**Honor System:** Community over individual (Mulaguun and Orowai). Strength proves rightness (Tembokarr). Ancestral wisdom guides the living (universal).
**scan_depth:** 3

### CULTURE -- The Cult of the Card
**Type:** Religious/spiritual movement
**Belief:** Spirit Cards are fragments of the divine — pieces of whatever force caused the Awakening. Using cards is communion with that force.
**Followers:** Present on all continents but strongest in Vessara (Khambattar especially). Considered fringe by most.
**Practices:** Card meditation (communing with equipped cards), card burial ceremonies (for destroyed cards), and the belief that Animated Holo cards are direct messages from the divine.
**Controversy:** Mainstream religions view the Cult as heretical. The Cult views mainstream religion as in denial about the true nature of the Awakening.
**Political Impact:** Minimal but growing. Some rulers quietly support the Cult because it reinforces the sacred nature of card ownership — and by extension, the power of those who hold the most cards.
**scan_depth:** 3

### CULTURE -- Attitudes Toward Hunters
**Type:** Social perception
**Reverence:** Hunters are the thin line between civilization and extinction. They are celebrated in stories, songs, and festivals. Children aspire to be hunters.
**Pity:** The mortality rate is known. Every hunter is someone who will probably die young. Families with multiple hunters are viewed with a mixture of admiration and sorrow.
**Fear:** Powerful hunters with three strong cards are dangerous individuals. They could kill ordinary soldiers. They could take what they want. This creates an undercurrent of fear.
**Exploitation:** Guilds, merchants, and rulers exploit hunters economically. High taxes, low pay relative to risk, and the constant pressure to produce cards.
**Division:** Full hunters (3 cards) are treated as elite. Apprentices and journeymen are respected but not revered. Failed hunters (those who quit or were injured) are pitied or ignored.
**scan_depth:** 3

### CULTURE -- Death and the Wilds
**Type:** Existential philosophy
**The Wilds as Death:** Outside the walls is death. This is not metaphor — it is practical truth. Every expedition carries the expectation of casualties.
**Acceptance:** Most people have made peace with mortality. Death is common enough that grief is brief and practical. Funerals are efficient. Life continues.
**The Good Death:** Dying in combat with a creature, protecting others, is the best death. Dying of disease or old age is acceptable. Dying of cowardice or betrayal is the worst death.
**The Body in the Wild:** Bodies left in the wilds are considered consumed by the world. There is no obligation to retrieve them. Doing so is an act of love, not duty.
**Children and Death:** Children are taught about death early. Sheltering them from the reality is considered irresponsible. A child who doesn't understand the walls by age 5 is considered at risk.
**scan_depth:** 3

### CULTURE -- Music, Art, and Storytelling
**Type:** Cultural expression
**Music:** Percussion-heavy. Drums signal wall alarms, marching rhythms, and celebrations. String instruments for mourning. Wind instruments for storytelling. Each continent has distinct musical traditions.
**Art:** Practical and memorial. Wall carvings document battles and heroes. Tapestry tradition in Essenhall. Ink painting in Vessara. Body art and scarification in Nolruun.
**Storytelling:** The primary entertainment. Oral tradition is strong everywhere. Professional storytellers in taverns and markets. Stories about the First Hunt, the Awakening, and legendary hunters are universal.
**Card Art:** Cards themselves have no imagery, but the cultural depiction of cards is rich. Paintings, sculptures, and jewelry inspired by card aesthetics are common.
**Literature:** Limited by literacy rates. Religious texts, practical manuals (farming, forging, combat), and personal journals. Fiction is rare but treasured.
**scan_depth:** 3

### CULTURE -- Food and Agriculture Within Walls
**Type:** Sustenance culture
**Staple Foods:** Grain porridge (universal), root vegetables, beans. Rice in Vessara. Mushroom varieties in Mulaguun. Preserved fish in coastal cities.
**Meat:** A luxury. Hunting provides meat but it is expensive. Most families eat meat once a week at most. Hunters eat better than most.
**Indoor Farming:** Vertical gardens on inner walls. Rooftop plots. Underground fungus farms (especially Mulaguun). Hydroponic experiments in Vessara. All limited by space and light.
**Livestock:** Chickens and pigs kept indoors. Goats in mountain cities. No cattle — too large for walled spaces. Feed is expensive, making livestock a significant investment.
**Cooking Traditions:** Slow-cooked stews (fuel-efficient), flatbreads (simple, filling), fermented vegetables (preservation). Spices are luxury trade goods. Bland food is the norm.
**Feast Days:** Special occasions warrant better food. Community meals on festivals. The hunter's share — tradition that hunters who return with meat share a portion with the nearest families.
**scan_depth:** 3

### CULTURE -- The Before Times (Nostalgia and Myth)
**Type:** Cultural memory
**The Before:** Life before the Awakening is remembered as a golden age — even though most of it was ordinary medieval existence. Memory idealizes what was lost.
**Generational Divide:** Those who remember the Before (fewer than 100 people alive worldwide, all elderly) speak of open fields, safe roads, and unmutated animals with wonder. Those born after the walls cannot truly imagine it.
**Artifacts:** Pre-Awakening objects carry emotional weight. Old maps showing roads between cities. Books describing a world without walls. Tools made with techniques lost to time.
**Knowledge Loss:** Each generation knows less about the world that was. Techniques, stories, and histories fade. Scholars fight a losing battle against ignorance.
**Mythologization:** The Before has become myth in some communities. Stories of flying machines and metal ships that some pre-Awakening books describe are dismissed as fantasy by most.
**The Dream:** Every culture has a version of the dream — that one day, the creatures will revert, the walls will come down, and humanity will reclaim the world. It is a dream, not a plan.
**scan_depth:** 3

### CULTURE -- Child Rearing and the Young
**Type:** Social structure
**Birth Rate:** Higher than pre-Awakening due to cultural pressure and need for population. Infant mortality is significant but declining with improved walled medicine.
**Education:** Practical. Reading and writing for the wealthy and guild members. Combat training for all. Survival skills taught from age 5. Card education begins at 10.
**Child Labor:** Common. Children work in family trades, farms, and workshops from young ages. Childhood as a protected period of play is a luxury of the wealthy.
**Orphans:** Numerous. Parent death from creatures, disease, and wall breaches creates many orphans. Orphanages are overcrowded and underfunded. Many orphans become hunters or criminals.
**Card Testing:** At age 12, children can attempt their first card equip. Testing is done with supervision using weak, safe cards. Success varies. Failure is not shameful — the vessels simply need more time.
**The Future:** Children represent hope and anxiety in equal measure. They are the next generation of hunters, leaders, and wall-maintainers. They are also the most likely to die in the next wall breach.
**scan_depth:** 3

### CULTURE -- Language and Communication
**Type:** Linguistic culture
**Common Tongue:** Each continent has a common language spoken by all city-states. Continental languages are mutually unintelligible.
**Dialects:** Significant dialect variation between cities within each continent. Hochveldt Germanic vs Dunncarrock Celtic-influenced. Tengzhai Mandarin-equivalent vs Surano Japanese-equivalent.
**Hunter Slang:** Universal terminology across all cultures. Three-soul, first/second/third slot, card drop, deck, burnout, cracked, the walk (death march into the wilds).
**Written Language:** Each continent has its own script. Literacy rates: 10-20% general population, 60-80% among guild members and officials, near-universal among nobility.
**Signal Systems:** Wall signals (fire, bell, horn), hunting hand signals (silent communication during expeditions), trade route markers (standardized signs indicating safe/dangerous zones).
**scan_depth:** 3

### CULTURE -- Pre-Awakening Knowledge and Lost Technology
**Type:** Intellectual culture

### POWER SYSTEM -- Card Acquisition and Drop Mechanics
**Type:** Loot system rules
**Drop Trigger:** A Spirit Card materializes within 1 meter of a slain mutated creature corpse. The process takes 3-10 seconds after death. The card appears with a faint shimmer of light.
**Drop Probability:** Varies by creature class. Vermin-tier: near 1%. Small predators: 2-5%. Large predators: 10-20%. Apex creatures: 30-50%. Legendary creatures: 90-100%.
**Variant Roll:** Independent of creature type. Normal: ~83%. Holo: ~14%. Animated Holo: ~3%. This roll is made separately from the drop roll. Sovereign cards do NOT follow this roll — see POWER SYSTEM -- Animated Holo Sovereign Cards for sovereign drop conditions.
**Multiple Drops:** A single creature produces at most one card. Groups of creatures can each drop independently.
**Condition:** The creature must be killed, not die of natural causes. Accidental deaths (falling, drowning) do not produce cards. The kill must involve deliberate action by a hunter or group.
**Corpse Condition:** The card materializes regardless of how damaged the corpse is. Even dismembered creatures can produce cards.
**Environmental Drop:** Cards do not degrade. A card that drops in water, mud, or lava remains intact. They are effectively indestructible when unequipped.
**scan_depth:** 5

### POWER SYSTEM -- Creature Danger Ratings
**Type:** Threat classification
**Scale:** 1 (nuisance, solo hunter trivial) to 10 (settlement-threatening, legendary expedition only).
**DR 1-2:** Vermin and small predators. Can kill an unprepared civilian but pose minimal threat to equipped hunters. Common near walls.
**DR 3-4:** Standard hunting targets. Require at least one experienced hunter. Pack versions elevate the threat. Most common expedition quarry.
**DR 5-6:** Serious threats. Require a coordinated party of 2-4 hunters. The upper end of what professional hunters face regularly.
**DR 7-8:** Apex predators. Require veteran parties with optimized loadouts. Encounters are rare and career-defining. Sovereign card candidates at DR 8.
**DR 9-10:** Legendary. Unique or near-unique creatures. Expeditions require months of planning. Return is not expected. The kind of creature that reshapes political maps if killed.
**Intelligence Note:** Most creatures are Instinctive (react to stimuli) or Responsive (basic pack coordination). Tactical creatures employ flanking, retreat-and-ambush, and target prioritization. Sapient-adjacent creatures understand tool use, trap avoidance, and grudges. Treat intelligence as a force multiplier — a DR 5 tactical creature is more dangerous than a DR 7 instinctive one.
