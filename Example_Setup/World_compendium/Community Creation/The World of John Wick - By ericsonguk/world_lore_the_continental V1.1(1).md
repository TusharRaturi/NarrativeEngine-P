# The Continental

## 1. WORLD OVERVIEW

### OVERVIEW — Core Premise
The public world believes governments, corporations, intelligence agencies, police forces, and organized crime compete through familiar institutions. Beneath that surface exists a second civilization: an old, international society of assassins, brokers, smugglers, cleaners, armorers, medics, couriers, crime families, and hotel keepers. It has its own currency, courts, sacred debts, neutral territories, and methods of execution. Its violence is hidden not because it is rare, but because thousands of professionals are paid to make it look like something else.

The player begins as a newly recognized professional assassin attached to a faction, family, intelligence program, or Continental sponsor. They have trained before. They may already have killed. What they have not yet done is complete a contract that places their name permanently in the Administration's ledger. Their first proper assignment is supposed to be controlled: one target, one city, one clean exit. Instead, it reveals that the target is connected to a Marker, a Table seat, a compromised intelligence program, or a private war that the player was never meant to see.

The campaign begins with work. It grows into obligation. Simple assassinations become courier runs, extractions, bodyguard details, counter-assassinations, false-flag operations, and diplomatic killings performed under rules no ordinary court recognizes. Every success increases the player's reputation and value. It also makes them visible to people who prefer useful assets to remain owned.

The central question is not whether the player can kill. It is whether they can remain a person while becoming excellent at it. They may obey the system, manipulate it, inherit a place within it, or become an anomaly hunted by everyone who fears another Baba Yaga.

**Genre:** Modern assassin noir, criminal conspiracy, espionage thriller, urban action.
**Tone:** GRITTY, NOIR, VIOLENT, ELEGANT, PARANOID, MORALLY SERIOUS.
**Era:** Contemporary modern world. No magic, cybernetics, science-fiction weapons, supernatural healing, or impossible technology.
**Core Themes:** Code and honor, debt, institutional violence, chosen family, professional identity, grief, loyalty, consequence, and the cost of freedom.
**Campaign Scale:** Begins with local contracts and personal rivalries; expands into international syndicate politics, intelligence operations, and conflicts involving seats of the High Table.

### OVERVIEW — The Open Ledger Continuity
The default campaign uses an **Open Ledger Continuity**. The recognizable institutions and characters of the Continental underworld exist, but the campaign is not locked to one exact point in film continuity. Characters who died in strict canon may appear alive, retired, missing, wounded, or active if doing so improves the campaign. Major events can be treated as rumors, alternate outcomes, or incidents whose precise truth was deliberately obscured by the Administration.

This allows John Wick, Winston Scott, Charon, the Bowery King, Sofia Al-Azwar, Caine, Koji Shimazu, Akira, the Director, Mr. Nobody, the Harbinger, and other known figures to coexist with original characters. The player is never required to reproduce a film plot. Canon is history, rumor, or pressure—not a railway track.

For a stricter campaign, the GM may select a specific film-era date and update character statuses accordingly. The engine should never assume a canon death unless the campaign explicitly establishes it.

### OVERVIEW — The Two Worlds
The civilian world is real and consequential. Police investigate. Cameras record. Hospitals ask questions. Border officials compare documents. Journalists notice patterns. Intelligence services collect anomalies. The underworld survives because it does not replace these institutions; it infiltrates, bribes, redirects, frightens, and overwhelms them selectively.

Most civilians know nothing. Some know fragments:
- A concierge who never asks why a guest is bleeding.
- A detective who has learned which cases are removed from the board.
- A surgeon who accepts unusual gold.
- A customs officer who stamps a black-edged passport without opening it.
- A journalist who has mapped twenty impossible deaths but cannot identify the organization connecting them.

The underworld's greatest protection is disbelief. Its rituals look theatrical because ritual creates consistency across languages and borders. A gold coin, a blood-sealed Marker, or a Continental crest communicates authority faster than a digital credential that can be copied or traced.

### OVERVIEW — The Laws That Hold the Underworld Together
The High Table's laws are not moral laws. They are rules designed to keep a civilization of professional killers from collapsing into constant war.

**No Business on Continental Grounds:** No killing, assault, coercive abduction, or contract execution inside a recognized Continental hotel or its formally declared sanctuary space. Violence in immediate self-defense may still be judged. The Manager determines the facts; the Table determines the punishment.

**A Marker Must Be Honored:** A blood-sealed Marker represents an absolute debt. The bearer may demand one service proportionate to the life-debt that created it. Refusal is punishable by death. Killing the holder merely transfers the problem to the Administration and stains the debtor as oathless.

**A Contract Must Be Sanctioned:** Unauthorized killings of protected underworld persons may trigger restitution, vendetta rights, or a bounty. Ordinary civilian murder is not automatically a Table concern, but it creates heat and may violate local faction rules.

**The Ledger Is Final:** Once the Administration records a contract, bounty, Marker, excommunication, or satisfaction of debt, the record becomes underworld law unless overturned by an Adjudicator, a Table seat, or a higher authority.

**Sanctuary Has Boundaries:** A Continental room, clinic, chapel, train carriage, or private salon is only neutral if formally recognized. A target cannot invent sanctuary by running into a crowded restaurant. Managers publish boundaries and can withdraw protection.

**The Dead Are Settled:** Contract ownership ends when proof is accepted and payment is released. Disputes over who delivered the final act are resolved by the Administration, not by gunfire in the payment hall.

**Consequences Must Be Witnessed:** Table punishment is public within the underworld. Excommunication, deconsecration, and formal execution are designed to teach everyone watching what the rule means.

### OVERVIEW — Player Role and Character Origins
The player is a capable but not legendary assassin. They begin with professional training, a limited network, a factional obligation, and one personal vulnerability. Their origin changes what doors open and which debts already exist.

Common origins include:
- **Ruska Roma Graduate:** Ballet discipline, wrestling, close-quarters violence, languages, and family obligation.
- **Continental Sponsored:** Trained through private tutors and introduced by a Manager who expects discretion.
- **Syndicate Soldier:** Raised inside a Bratva, Yakuza, Camorra, Triad, cartel, or other Table-aligned criminal family.
- **State Program Defector:** Former intelligence operative whose government erased them, sold them, or denied their existence.
- **Independent Contractor:** Self-taught or military-trained, recently vouched into the ledger by a broker.
- **Courier Turned Killer:** Knows routes, borders, codes, and faces; became violent because delivery alone stopped being enough.
- **Legacy Child:** Born to an assassin family and burdened by a reputation they did not earn.
- **Civilian Recruit:** A translator, medic, athlete, investigator, driver, or technician pulled into the underworld after surviving an incident.

At character creation, establish:
- The faction that trained or sponsors the player.
- The person who can still command their loyalty.
- The person they failed, abandoned, or killed.
- The one rule they believe they will never break.
- The one Marker, ticket, secret, or hidden identity that could destroy them.

### OVERVIEW — Campaign Opening: The First Proper Contract
The player's sponsor offers a contract intended to confirm their professional status. The assignment should initially seem direct:
- Eliminate a financier before he boards an international flight.
- Deliver a sealed case through a city during a transport strike.
- Extract a compromised accountant from a diplomatic reception.
- Protect a witness for one night without learning what they know.
- Recover a stolen Marker before it is registered.
- Kill a defector inside a criminal family without harming the family itself.

The first contract becomes complicated by three facts:
1. The stated target is not the true objective.
2. Another faction has hired a rival professional with different instructions.
3. Completing the job exactly as ordered will violate either the player's code, a hidden Marker, or the spirit of Continental law.

The first campaign arc should teach the rules before asking the player whether to break them.

### OVERVIEW — Contract Structure
Every contract has six operational layers:

**Principal:** The person or institution paying for the work. The true principal may be concealed behind brokers.

**Objective:** Assassination, extraction, escort, recovery, intimidation, sabotage, surveillance, or counter-operation.

**Target:** A person, object, secret, location, relationship, or scheduled event.

**Conditions:** Time limit, required method, protected persons, geographic restrictions, proof requirements, or secrecy standards.

**Interference:** Rival assassins, law enforcement, intelligence officers, family security, false information, or changes issued after deployment.

**Exit:** Payment, transport, cleanup, medical support, and who assumes responsibility if the operation becomes public.

A professional reads conditions more carefully than the target dossier. The easiest way to manipulate an assassin is to tell the truth incompletely.

### OVERVIEW — Common Contract Types
**Sanctioned Elimination:** Kill a named target and provide accepted proof. Method is usually open unless specified.

**Quiet Elimination:** Death must appear natural, accidental, self-inflicted, criminally unrelated, or impossible to connect to the principal.

**Public Message:** The target must die visibly, often with a token, phrase, or object left behind. These jobs create political consequences.

**Extraction:** Remove a person from hostile control and deliver them alive. Often harder than assassination because the asset has opinions, fear, and divided loyalties.

**Courier Run:** Deliver an object, message, Marker, account book, biological sample, or weapon without opening it. The courier may be deliberately kept ignorant.

**Recovery:** Retrieve stolen property, a body, a coin press, a ledger, a signet, or evidence before authorities or rivals secure it.

**Protective Detail:** Keep a principal alive through a defined window. Assassins hired as bodyguards must think defensively rather than hunt.

**Counter-Assassination:** Identify and neutralize a killer without harming the intended target, often under public conditions.

**Witness Management:** Persuade, relocate, discredit, or kill a witness. The chosen method affects reputation.

**Internal Correction:** Enforce faction discipline against one of its own. These jobs are personal, politically sensitive, and frequently designed as loyalty tests.

**False Flag:** Perform an operation that appears to belong to another faction or state. Discovery can start wars.

**Table Service:** A command issued through the Administration. Refusal may end a career; success may still create enemies.

### OVERVIEW — Reputation, Heat, and Standing
The player is tracked along three narrative pressures.

**Reputation:** What professionals believe the player can do. Reputation opens premium contracts, specialist services, invitations, and fear. It also attracts challengers and principals who consider the player expendable precisely because they are capable.

**Heat:** Attention from police, intelligence services, journalists, cameras, civilians, and local government. Heat is geographic and evidence-based. A player may be notorious in Berlin but legally invisible in Osaka.

**Standing:** The player's legal and social status inside the underworld. Standing reflects honored contracts, debts, Continental conduct, faction trust, and Adjudicator judgments. A feared killer can have excellent reputation and ruined standing.

The three pressures should not be merged. Killing ten people in public may increase reputation while raising heat and reducing standing.

### OVERVIEW — Narrative Style and GM Principles
The world is heightened but not magical. Characters may display extraordinary training, pain tolerance, accuracy, and resolve, but their bodies remain human.

**Violence Is Fast and Expensive:** Ammunition runs out. Armor deforms. Hands shake. Blood loss matters. A knife in the wrong place ends a career.

**Preparation Beats Power:** Floor plans, clothing, language, transport, timing, and social access matter more than exotic equipment.

**Every Room Has a Social Rule:** A nightclub, church, hotel, embassy, train, bathhouse, gallery, and family dinner each create different constraints on violence.

**Competence Is Attractive, Not Invincibility:** NPCs should feel dangerous because they make good decisions, not because bullets cease to affect them.

**The Code Is Always Under Pressure:** Rules matter most when obeying them costs something.

**No Comedy World:** Dry wit and human warmth are allowed, but violence is not slapstick and death is not a punchline.

**No Supernatural Escape:** There is no resurrection, magic antidote, impossible hacking, or unexplained gadget that solves the operation.

**Civilians Are Not Scenery:** Harm to bystanders affects characters, factions, heat, and the moral texture of the campaign.

**Enemies Have Lives:** A target may be cruel, sympathetic, terrified, guilty, innocent of the stated accusation, or all of these at once.

### OVERVIEW — Core Moral Questions
- Does a code make killing civilized, or merely organized?
- Is an honored debt still honorable when the requested act is monstrous?
- Can loyalty survive when every institution treats people as assets?
- Is freedom possible without becoming dangerous to everyone who remains inside the system?
- When does professional detachment become moral cowardice?
- Who deserves mercy in a world where mercy creates witnesses?
- Is the player becoming a legend, or becoming impossible to love?

## 2. FACTIONS

### FACTION — The High Table
**Type:** Supranational Criminal Sovereignty / Council of Twelve
**Key Members:** The twelve seated representatives, the Marquis-appointed emissaries, hereditary secretaries, regional chamberlains
**Stance:** Absolute authority; stability above justice
The High Table is the closest thing the underworld has to a world government. Twelve seats represent dynasties, syndicates, old criminal houses, and transnational power blocs. The Table does not manage every crime. It governs the rules that allow criminal empires and professional killers to coexist without destroying their markets.

The Table controls recognition, sanctuary charters, coin authority, Marker registration, inter-faction arbitration, excommunication, and the legitimacy of large bounties. It commands enormous financial resources but prefers to act through existing institutions. When it deploys its own Enforcers, the action is meant to be unmistakable.

The Table's central contradiction is that it claims timeless authority while constantly changing the rules to protect whichever seats currently dominate. Some members believe the code is sacred. Others see it as a brand around naked power. Rival seats use assassins as votes conducted with bullets.

**Player Relation:** Ultimate employer, regulator, and potential enemy. The Table can grant recognition, erase protection, elevate a patron, or turn every professional in a city toward the player.

### FACTION — The Continental Hotel Network
**Type:** Neutral Sanctuary Network / Diplomatic Hospitality Order
**Key Members:** Winston Scott, Charon, Koji Shimazu, Sofia Al-Azwar, regional Managers and concierges
**Stance:** Neutral by charter; privately political
The Continental is a global network of luxury hotels serving the recognized underworld. Each property is independently managed but bound to a shared charter. Its staff provide rooms, secure communications, medical referrals, tailoring, transport, weapons introductions, archives, and diplomatic meeting space.

Every chartered Continental maintains a local **service constellation** rather than one standardized shop. An armorer may operate as a sommelier, antiquarian, sporting outfitter, gallery curator, gunsmith, or quartermaster depending on the city. Medical care may be hidden inside a private clinic, bathhouse, cosmetic-surgery practice, monastery ward, or after-hours physician's office. Tailors, document specialists, drivers, cleaners, couriers, and intelligence brokers are introduced through the concierge according to the guest's standing and the needs of the contract. This makes each hotel a gateway into its city rather than a universal vending machine.

Continental services are privileges, not entitlements. Routine introductions may be settled with Coins. Rare equipment, politically dangerous access, emergency surgery, restricted documents, or a hurried extraction usually require additional payment, a returned favor, sponsorship, or a record placed in the hotel's private ledger. Managers may deny a service without explaining why. Weapons crossing sanctuary are sealed, secured, or escorted according to local practice; test firing and arming occur beyond consecrated space.

The sacred rule is simple: no business on Continental grounds. The complexity lies in defining grounds, business, self-defense, conspiracy, and provocation. Managers survive by understanding where written law ends and institutional discretion begins.

Every Continental has its own character. New York values old-world ceremony and personal loyalty. Osaka blends family obligation with disciplined security. Rome treats assassins like aristocratic pilgrims. Casablanca operates more like a fortified diplomatic port. London hides brutality behind club manners and legal precision.

Internal tension exists between Managers who believe sanctuary protects civilization and those who use neutrality to accumulate personal power.

**Player Relation:** Safehouse, social hub, service center, rumor market, and political arena. The hotel protects the player only while the player protects the institution's legitimacy.

### FACTION — The Administration
**Type:** Bureaucratic Contract Registry / Underworld Communications Authority
**Key Members:** The Chief Operator, tattooed switchboard clerks, verification officers, proof examiners, Marker registrars
**Stance:** Procedurally neutral; loyal to the ledger
The Administration records contracts, bounties, sanctioned deaths, payments, Marker obligations, excommunications, reinstatements, and official notices. Its operators favor typewriters, paper cards, pneumatic tubes, chalkboards, mechanical stamps, and closed analog lines. These methods are not nostalgia. They reduce remote intrusion and force conspirators to compromise people rather than systems.

The Administration does not decide who deserves to die. It decides whether the order is valid, who owns it, whether conditions were satisfied, and which notice the underworld receives. A single clerical delay can save a life. A deliberately misplaced card can start a war.

Operators are considered noncombatant while performing official duty, though the Table has executed staff for altering records. Some clerks quietly trade timing information. Others treat the ledger as a sacred text.

**Player Relation:** Source of contracts, bounty status, Marker verification, and official proof. The player may need an operator's help long before they need a larger gun.

### FACTION — The Adjudicators and Enforcers
**Type:** Judicial Emissaries / High Table Enforcement Arm
**Key Members:** Senior Adjudicators, the Zero Office, armored Enforcer captains, sanction auditors
**Stance:** Punitive, formal, politically selective
Adjudicators investigate violations of Table law, assign restitution, depose Managers, deconsecrate properties, and pronounce excommunication. They arrive with records, witnesses, seals, and enough force to ensure their interpretation becomes reality.

Enforcers are disciplined tactical personnel equipped with modern body armor, carbines, breaching tools, shields, communications gear, and specialized anti-assassin doctrine. They are not anonymous waves of disposable soldiers. Their teams isolate exits, control lighting, deny ammunition, protect medics, and force fugitives into predictable routes.

The faction's internal conflict is between legalists who want evidence and political instruments sent with a verdict already decided. Ambitious Adjudicators may provoke violations to justify dismantling a rival institution.

**Player Relation:** Investigators, hunters, judges, and campaign antagonists when the player's actions threaten the code or embarrass a Table seat.

### FACTION — The Bowery
**Type:** Off-Grid Intelligence and Logistics Network
**Key Members:** The Bowery King, pigeon handlers, station masters, street captains, volunteer medics
**Stance:** Defiant, transactional, anti-monopoly
The Bowery hides inside the population that powerful people train themselves not to see. Its members control rooftops, alleys, shelters, service tunnels, loading docks, abandoned stations, and informal economies. Messages move by pigeon, handwritten note, trusted runner, and rotary line.

The Bowery knows who entered a building, which alley camera is fake, where a wounded killer bought aspirin, and which police patrol is avoiding a block. It lacks the Table's formal legitimacy but possesses the kind of local truth that central authorities cannot manufacture.

The network is not a charity. It protects its own, sells information selectively, and remembers disrespect. Some members want independence from all elite killers; others are loyal specifically to the King and his wars.

**Player Relation:** Source of unregistered weapons, street intelligence, hidden movement, emergency care, and jobs the Table should not know exist.

### FACTION — The Ruska Roma
**Type:** Criminal Family / Cultural Order / Assassin Academy
**Key Members:** The Director, senior ballet masters, wrestling coaches, archivists, family elders
**Stance:** Traditional, protective, severe
The Ruska Roma raises artists and killers under the same roof. Ballet creates balance, endurance, repetition, and the ability to perform through pain. Wrestling and combat training teach leverage, pressure, and survival at intimate distance. Language, history, etiquette, and music prevent students from becoming mere weapons, though the institution still expects obedience.

Membership is family, identity, and debt. Tickets and rosaries carry recognized authority. A graduate may request passage or assistance, but every favor becomes part of the family memory.

The Director protects the institution by making brutal choices. Internal tension divides those who believe tradition preserves displaced people from those who believe the school consumes children to maintain influence.

**Player Relation:** Strong origin faction, source of training and emergency refuge, and a demanding family that may call the player home for work they cannot refuse.

### FACTION — The Tarasov Bratva
**Type:** Russian Organized Crime Syndicate / Table-Aligned Enterprise
**Key Members:** Viggo Tarasov, surviving captains, port directors, nightclub owners, fuel and construction fronts
**Stance:** Expansionist, pragmatic, reputation-conscious
The Tarasov organization built influence through ports, construction, fuel, vehicle theft, protection, and disciplined violence. Its leadership understands that fear is useful only when paired with predictable business. The Bratva hires elite assassins when internal soldiers would create unacceptable political exposure.

Its captains compete constantly. Some respect Continental law because it protects revenue. Others consider the code an obstacle that rich men invoke selectively. A succession crisis can transform routine work into a civil war fought across restaurants, bathhouses, warehouses, and family homes.

**Player Relation:** Frequent employer, origin faction, target pool, or rival patron. Tarasov work pays well but often contains family politics omitted from the dossier.

### FACTION — The Camorra and D’Antonio House
**Type:** Italian Crime Dynasty / High Society Syndicate
**Key Members:** Santino D’Antonio, Gianna D’Antonio, chamberlains, Vatican intermediaries, Roman brokers
**Stance:** Aristocratic, ambitious, ceremonial
The D’Antonio house treats underworld power as inherited nobility. It controls finance, art storage, construction, political access, and old European routes. Its members use Markers with legalistic precision and employ ceremony to make coercion appear consensual.

The central conflict is succession. Every inheritance produces factions among accountants, soldiers, priests, cousins, and lovers. Camorra operations favor social infiltration, poisoned alliances, and assassinations performed at culturally meaningful moments.

**Player Relation:** Source of elegant, morally compromising contracts. The house may offer advancement, patronage, or a Marker that turns success into lifelong ownership.

### FACTION — Osaka’s Shimazu House
**Type:** Yakuza-Aligned Family / Continental Stewardship
**Key Members:** Koji Shimazu, Akira Shimazu, senior swordsmen, hotel security captains
**Stance:** Honor-bound, locally protective, skeptical of distant authority
The Shimazu house protects the Osaka Continental and maintains relationships with Japanese criminal clans, business families, martial schools, and municipal power brokers. Its culture values direct loyalty and personal accountability more than abstract obedience to foreign Table politics.

The house trains firearms, sword, knife, archery, and close-quarters tactics without treating any weapon as sacred. Family members are expected to understand hospitality as seriously as combat.

Its vulnerability is loyalty itself. Enemies know Koji will risk institutional security for people he considers family. Younger members disagree over whether honor means obedience, resistance, or survival.

**Player Relation:** Ally, employer, training connection, or refuge in East Asia. A player accepted by the Shimazu may inherit enemies they have never met.

### FACTION — The Casablanca Compact
**Type:** North African Continental Domain / Trade and Intelligence Nexus
**Key Members:** Sofia Al-Azwar, kennel masters, port brokers, desert guides, customs intermediaries
**Stance:** Protective, independent, transactional
The Casablanca Compact connects European, African, Mediterranean, and Middle Eastern routes. It specializes in port movement, forged manifests, animal handling, desert transport, and intelligence gathered through trade rather than surveillance systems.

The Compact's trained dogs are working partners, not disposable weapons. Handlers build commands, trust, and battlefield coordination over years. Harming one carries personal and professional consequences.

The faction balances Continental obligations against local sovereignty. Some brokers want closer Table protection; others believe the Table's presence attracts wars that Casablanca has survived by avoiding.

**Player Relation:** International transit, tracking support, smuggling, desert passage, and difficult favors from people who remember exactly how they were treated.

### FACTION — The Jade Meridian
**Type:** East Asian Triad and Broker Confederation
**Key Members:** Madam Lin Yue, accountant Chen Bo, Macau gaming brokers, Hong Kong shipping captains
**Stance:** Commercial, discreet, internally divided
The Jade Meridian is a coalition of Triad societies, financial brokers, shipping firms, and family enterprises that coordinate under a shared Table seat. It excels at moving money, people, and information through dense commercial systems without creating obvious disruption.

Its assassins favor access: kitchens, service elevators, private gaming rooms, luxury retail corridors, ferries, and family celebrations. Violence is considered a failure of positioning unless the violence itself is the message.

The confederation's weakness is generational conflict. Older leaders trust kinship and face-to-face obligation. Younger operators want data, corporate structures, and global expansion. Both sides hire outsiders because outsiders can be blamed.

**Player Relation:** Employer for subtle urban operations, source of forged commercial identities, or rival in contracts involving ports and finance.

### FACTION — The Santa Muerte Compact
**Type:** Cartel Alliance / Religious-Fraternal Criminal Order
**Key Members:** Doña Marisol Vega, Father Tomás, border captains, sicario instructors
**Stance:** Devotional, ruthless, community-entangled
The Compact controls narcotics routes, agricultural fronts, border movement, private airstrips, and protection economies across parts of the Americas. Its members blend family loyalty, folk devotion, practical generosity, and extreme retaliation.

The Compact funds clinics, funerals, schools, and local defense where the state is absent, while also creating the violence those services treat. Its killers understand rural terrain, convoy warfare, ambush, and human intelligence.

Internal tension separates community traditionalists from executives who want to become a multinational corporation with a religious logo.

**Player Relation:** High-paying rural and cross-border contracts, convoy work, sanctuary with strings, and enemies capable of reaching extended family.

### FACTION — The Directorate
**Type:** Deniable State Intelligence Program / Asset Management Office
**Key Members:** Director Evelyn Ward, case officer Gabriel March, training commandants, parliamentary liaison officers
**Stance:** Officially nonexistent; utilitarian
The Directorate is a Western intelligence black program built to recruit, train, condition, and deploy deniable operatives. It uses psychological assessment, identity reconstruction, language immersion, surveillance detection, and controlled trauma. Unlike the Table, it claims national security rather than tradition.

The program has discovered that underworld infrastructure is often more reliable than government logistics. It quietly purchases Continental services while publicly denying the criminal world exists.

Directorate operatives are not automatically assassins. They conduct surveillance, extraction, sabotage, influence operations, and targeted killing. Retired assets are rarely allowed to remain retired if they know program architecture.

Internal tension divides officers who still believe in public service from careerists who use secret authority to settle institutional and personal scores.

**Player Relation:** Possible origin, employer, rival intelligence service, or source of files proving that the player's entire identity was manufactured.

### FACTION — Maison Noire
**Type:** Private Finishing School / Espionage and Assassination Brokerage
**Key Members:** Madame Celeste Armand, instructor Mara Kade, patron council, placement agents
**Stance:** Cultivated, predatory, commercially neutral
Maison Noire recruits exceptional young adults from intelligence schools, military academies, prisons, elite sports, foster systems, and criminal families. It teaches languages, etiquette, seduction awareness, surveillance, poison recognition, firearms, escape, and controlled violence. Its public front is a network of arts foundations and diplomatic training institutes.

Students are not stripped of identity through science-fiction conditioning. They are shaped through debt, isolation, reward, social engineering, and carefully designed dependency. Graduates are placed with states, corporations, syndicates, or private patrons.

Some instructors believe they save talented people from worse futures. Others treat graduates as luxury products. A quiet reform faction helps operatives disappear.

**Player Relation:** Origin option, rival school, source of elegant infiltrators, or institution holding files on the player's former life.

### FACTION — The Glass House
**Type:** Private Intelligence Corporation / Corporate Espionage Service
**Key Members:** CEO Adrian Vale, operations chief Helena Frost, legal director Simon Kessler
**Stance:** Corporate, deniable, aggressively apolitical
The Glass House sells intelligence, counterintelligence, executive protection, sabotage, and discreet violence to corporations and wealthy individuals. It recruits former state operatives who want higher pay and fewer ideological constraints.

Its strength is integration. Lawyers, investigators, accountants, surveillance teams, lobbyists, and shooters work from the same objective plan. The company often wins without firing by freezing accounts, exposing affairs, manipulating boards, or triggering regulatory raids.

The Table distrusts the Glass House because it wants underworld services without accepting underworld sovereignty. The Glass House believes every code can be converted into a contractual risk premium.

**Player Relation:** Modern rival, employer for corporate operations, or enemy capable of attacking the player's civilian cover instead of their body.

### FACTION — The Courier Compact
**Type:** Neutral Delivery Guild / Cross-Border Logistics Order
**Key Members:** Idris Quill, route masters, diplomatic drivers, rail conductors, dead-drop custodians
**Stance:** Neutral while carrying; unforgiving when obstructed
The Courier Compact transports messages, Markers, coins, account books, keys, evidence, and small high-value objects. A courier does not open a sealed package and does not reveal a principal. Their neutrality is narrower than Continental sanctuary but respected because every faction needs reliable delivery.

Couriers memorize routes, border habits, train schedules, ferry crews, customs weaknesses, and emergency dead drops. Some are excellent drivers and fighters, but their first skill is avoidance.

The Compact maintains a death clause: killing a recognized courier carrying sealed material triggers collective refusal of service and a recovery bounty.

**Player Relation:** Escort contracts, delivery work, emergency transport, and access to people who know how every city connects.

### FACTION — The Cleaners’ Guild
**Type:** Forensic Erasure and Scene Management Cooperative
**Key Members:** Dr. Samir Vale, regional foremen, disposal specialists, bribery accountants
**Stance:** Neutral, expensive, clinically practical
The Cleaners remove bodies, blood, casings, digital traces, witnesses who agree to relocation, and evidence that can be redirected. They also construct alternate explanations: gang disputes, burglary, overdose, electrical fire, domestic violence, or simple disappearance.

They are not miracle workers. A public massacre with hundreds of phones cannot be erased. It can only be confused, fragmented, and politically buried. Cleaners charge according to exposure, not body count.

The Guild keeps private records as insurance against clients who refuse payment. Its greatest internal debate concerns where cleanup ends and murder begins.

**Player Relation:** Essential aftermath service, source of forensic insight, and dangerous creditor if the player cannot pay.

### FACTION — The Elder’s Court
**Type:** Mythic-Political Authority / Desert Sovereignty
**Key Members:** The Elder, pathfinders, water keepers, oath witnesses, former exiles
**Stance:** Remote, selective, superior to ordinary Table procedure
The Elder's Court exists beyond normal bureaucracy. Its authority is partly legal and partly sustained by collective belief. Those who reach it may seek absolution, altered obligation, or recognition above the Table—but reaching it requires guides, endurance, sacrifice, and proof that the petitioner offers something worth changing the order for.

The Court does not provide easy escape. Absolution creates a new duty. A severed finger, surrendered name, impossible service, or permanent exile may be demanded as visible proof.

Some Table members believe the Elder is a necessary spiritual authority. Others suspect the office survives because no seat wants to test whether its power is real.

**Player Relation:** Late-campaign destination for absolution, radical renegotiation, or discovery that freedom granted by a ruler is still a form of ownership.

## 3. LOCATIONS

### LOCATION — The New York Continental
**Type:** Luxury Hotel / Neutral Sanctuary / Political Court
**Status:** Flourishing, watched, politically strained
The New York Continental occupies an old building whose public elegance conceals reinforced service corridors, private elevators, secure rooms, concealed medical access, and staff trained to notice weapons without staring. Its lobby is a theater of controlled neutrality: assassins share drinks with former targets, brokers conduct negotiations beneath chandeliers, and the concierge remembers every face.

The most dangerous place is often the bar. Everyone is armed. Nobody may act. A conversation can become more threatening because violence is forbidden. The basement archive contains guest records, unpaid accounts, and incidents that could embarrass several Table seats.

**Weapons — The Reserve:** The concierge may arrange a private tasting at the Reserve, a cellar beneath an old wine-import house connected to the hotel by a secured service route. The Sommelier does not begin with a catalogue. They ask where the work will occur, what the target is likely to wear, how the weapon must enter, and whether the player expects to leave with it. Handguns, compact long guns, shotguns, blades, suppressors, ammunition, breaching tools, and protective equipment are presented as mission pairings. Unusual requests create a record and may require sponsorship. Purchases leave the hotel sealed and are opened only after the guest crosses the sanctuary boundary.

## 5. POWER SYSTEM & RULES

### POWER_SYSTEM — Professional Competence and Human Limits
There are no supernatural powers. A professional's apparent “power” comes from training, preparation, reputation, access, pain tolerance, and the ability to make decisions while other people panic.

The setting uses cinematic realism rather than documentary simulation. Elite assassins can perform exceptionally difficult feats, fight through injury for a limited time, and maintain accuracy under pressure. They cannot ignore anatomy, indefinitely outrun exhaustion, heal overnight, or survive direct trauma without consequence.

The engine should judge action through five questions:
1. **Position:** Does the player have cover, distance, surprise, or social access?
2. **Preparation:** Did they obtain information, tools, clothing, transport, and an exit plan?
3. **Competence:** Does their background support the attempted method?
4. **Pressure:** Are they injured, exhausted, rushed, emotionally compromised, or exposed?
5. **Consequence:** Even if the action succeeds, what evidence, injury, debt, or enemy does it create?

A highly trained player can defeat several ordinary opponents by controlling space and tempo. Several trained opponents coordinating intelligently remain lethal. Legendary figures are extraordinary because they repeatedly create advantage, not because they possess more health than other humans.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Core Professional Disciplines
Characters may specialize, but no professional is perfect at everything.

**Gunwork:** Safe handling, draw speed, recoil control, movement, retention shooting, transitions, reloads, use of cover, and target discrimination.

**Close Quarters:** Wrestling, judo, sambo, boxing, knife work, weapon retention, clinch fighting, improvised tools, and control in confined spaces.

**Mobility:** Driving, motorcycles, parkour, climbing, swimming, route selection, physical conditioning, and movement through crowds.

**Tradecraft:** Surveillance detection, disguise through behavior, dead drops, covert entry, countersurveillance, document use, and maintaining cover stories.

**Social Access:** Etiquette, negotiation, deception, intimidation, seduction awareness, languages, reading status, and knowing when not to speak.

**Underworld Knowledge:** Coins, Markers, Continental rules, faction symbols, broker etiquette, proof standards, and criminal geography.

**Field Medicine:** Hemorrhage control, airway management, splinting, shock recognition, drug risks, and evacuation decisions. Field medicine stabilizes; it does not replace surgery.

**Resolve:** Functioning through fear, grief, pain, isolation, and moral injury. Resolve does not prevent trauma. It determines whether the character can act before processing it.

A player should begin strong in two disciplines, competent in two, and limited in the rest. Improvement comes through training, hard experience, mentorship, and scars—not numeric superhuman escalation.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Preparation Advantage
Preparation is the closest thing this world has to magic. Before a mission, the player can create advantages through:
- Floor plans and service schedules.
- Clothing appropriate to role and weather.
- Local language and etiquette.
- Target routines and emotional vulnerabilities.
- Concealed tools selected for actual access conditions.
- Alternate transport and a clean identity.
- Medical and cleanup arrangements.
- Bribed staff, sympathetic insiders, or planted rumors.
- Pre-positioned weapons, vehicles, clothing, and cash.
- A decision about what to abandon if the plan fails.

Preparation should create options rather than guarantee success. Intelligence may be outdated or intentionally false. A contact may panic. A building may renovate an exit. A target may change routine because another assassin has already made contact.

The GM should reward specific planning. “I prepare” is not enough; the player states what they learn or arrange and accepts the cost.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Continental Procurement and Service Houses
Continental hotels do not contain universal equipment shops. They maintain trusted local networks whose methods reflect the city, its laws, and the Manager's politics. A guest begins by explaining the operational problem to the concierge or service master. The network then offers an introduction, not a guarantee.

**Standing Determines Access:** A recognized guest with clean standing can obtain routine rooms, tailoring, transport, basic medical referral, and ordinary equipment. Restricted weapons, rare ammunition, politically sensitive documents, emergency extraction, or specialist teams require stronger reputation, a sponsoring faction, a Marker, or a direct Manager's authorization.

**Mission Fit Comes Before Prestige:** Armorers ask about environment, target protection, concealment, distance, weather, transport, and exit. A famous weapon may be refused if it is badly suited to the work. The setting rewards selecting a workable kit rather than collecting trophies.

**Coins Pay for Service; Debts Pay for Impossibility:** A small number of Coins may cover routine professional hospitality. Urgent surgery, a bespoke armored wardrobe, an unregistered long gun, a diplomatic invitation, or a vehicle waiting across a border may create a separate monetary fee or a favor recorded in the local ledger.

**Loaned Equipment Has a History:** Some weapons and vehicles belong to the service house. They are expected to be returned, destroyed under instruction, or accounted for. Abandoning registered equipment can expose the supplier and reduce the player's Standing even when the mission succeeds.

**Sanctuary Handling:** Weapons entering a Continental are unloaded, sealed, checked, or escorted according to local custom. An armorer may display and fit equipment within a protected service room, but live testing and operational arming occur beyond sanctuary unless the Manager declares an emergency.

**Forensic and Political Cost:** A weapon is not only a damage tool. It has origin, ammunition, maintenance history, legal status, and potential links to previous work. Highly distinctive equipment can create Reputation while also increasing Heat. Local service masters know which choices will cause civilian authorities, intelligence agencies, or rival factions to take unusual interest.

**Supporting Services Matter:** Tailors create protection and believable social roles. Medics preserve life but create recovery obligations. Intelligence brokers sell confidence levels rather than omniscience. Drivers and couriers provide movement but may refuse improvisation. Cleaners reduce evidence; they do not erase every witness or institution.

The GM should make procurement a scene when the choices reveal character, create debt, establish local culture, or materially change the operation. Routine replacements may be handled briefly once a service relationship is established.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Firearms and Ammunition
Firearms are modern, mechanical, and limited by concealment, maintenance, ammunition, recoil, noise, legal environment, and access.

**Handguns:** Concealable and practical at close range. Limited capacity and difficult precision under movement. A common professional choice because the weapon can be carried into spaces where a rifle cannot.

**Submachine Guns and Compact Carbines:** Effective in confined environments but harder to conceal and politically conspicuous. Require planning to transport.

**Rifles:** Dominant at distance and against armor, but create immediate civilian and police escalation. Carrying one changes the nature of the operation.

**Shotguns:** Devastating at short range with appropriate ammunition. Long, heavy, loud, and difficult to hide. Door breaching risks injury and announces the operation.

**Suppressors:** Reduce muzzle blast and alter sound. They do not make gunfire silent. Supersonic ammunition still cracks. Mechanical noise, impacts, and witnesses remain.

**Special Ammunition:** Armor-piercing, subsonic, frangible, barrier-blind, and specialty loads exist but create supply, reliability, legal, and forensic consequences. A professional tests ammunition in the actual weapon.

**Reloads:** Magazines are finite. A character under pressure may lose partially loaded magazines, fumble, or choose not to retrieve them. Ammunition should matter without turning every scene into accounting.

**Weapon Failure:** Well-maintained modern firearms are reliable, but poor ammunition, damaged magazines, fouling, blood, impact, or improper grip can cause failure. The GM uses failure when circumstances support it, not as arbitrary punishment.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Blades, Bows, and Improvised Weapons
Blades remain relevant because they are quiet, concealable, reliable, and effective in grappling range. They are also dangerous to the user. Knife fights commonly injure both participants.

Swords and larger blades are culturally and operationally specific. They offer reach and psychological effect but are difficult to conceal. A swordsman in a modern city must plan transport and access.

Bows and crossbows can be quiet and effective in controlled conditions but are not universal stealth weapons. They require space, line of sight, retrieval planning, and skill. Specialized archers are dangerous because they build operations around the weapon rather than carrying it for style.

Improvised weapons—belts, bottles, pens, tools, furniture, cables, vehicle doors—are situational. They create brief advantage, not comedy. The environment matters because professional violence often begins before a preferred weapon is available.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Armor and Tailoring
Ballistic tailoring is a major underworld service. Protective suits use layered fabrics and discreet inserts to reduce penetration and fragmentation from common handgun threats.

Armor rules:
- Armor does not make bullets harmless. Impact causes bruising, fractures, internal injury, disorientation, and loss of breath.
- Repeated hits degrade protection and the wearer's ability to function.
- Seams, limbs, neck, head, and lower torso remain vulnerable.
- Rifle rounds and specialty ammunition can defeat soft armor.
- Rigid plates offer better protection but add bulk, weight, limited coverage, and obvious tactical appearance.
- Blades may exploit gaps and can penetrate some soft armor through concentrated force.
- A damaged suit must be inspected and repaired or replaced.

Armor is most useful because it turns immediate death into injury and a decision. It does not eliminate consequence.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Wounds, Pain, and Recovery
Injuries persist across scenes and may alter campaign choices.

**Minor Trauma:** Cuts, bruises, shallow wounds, sprains, burns, and mild concussion symptoms. Can be treated quickly but worsen with continued exertion.

**Serious Trauma:** Gunshot wounds without major organ damage, deep lacerations, fractures, dislocations, significant blood loss, and major concussion. Requires professional treatment and days to months of recovery.

**Critical Trauma:** Major vessel injury, organ damage, penetrating chest or abdominal wounds, severe head trauma, spinal injury, or extensive burns. Immediate stabilization and surgery are required. Survival may create permanent limitations.

**Adrenaline Window:** A character may function briefly after severe injury, particularly when focused on escape or protection. The cost arrives afterward through collapse, shock, impaired judgment, and longer recovery.

**Pain Medication:** Helps function but impairs perception, coordination, or judgment depending on drug and dose. Mixing substances is dangerous.

**Recovery:** Rest, nutrition, surgery, rehabilitation, and time matter. The GM should use recovery scenes for relationships, political vulnerability, nightmares, and contracts the player cannot personally control.

**Scars:** Physical scars affect movement, appearance, cover identities, and memory. Psychological trauma may create avoidance, hypervigilance, emotional numbness, or fixation. Trauma is not a random insanity effect; it is a human response that should be portrayed with restraint.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Death and Surrender
Death is final. A named character should not die casually, but no plot armor makes them immortal.

Professionals sometimes surrender. Surrender may lead to interrogation, ransom, recruitment, Marker coercion, exchange, or formal execution. Killing a surrendered person can be strategically correct and morally consequential. Accepting surrender creates transport and containment problems.

A defeated player may survive through:
- A faction claiming ownership.
- A rival needing information.
- Continental staff intervening at a boundary.
- A Marker holder demanding the player's life be preserved.
- A principal changing the objective.
- An enemy choosing humiliation, recruitment, or exchange over death.

Survival should create a debt or altered situation, never a free reset.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Surveillance and Technology
Technology is contemporary and fallible.

Cameras, facial recognition, license-plate readers, financial records, phone metadata, building access logs, and social media create serious exposure. The underworld counters them through stolen credentials, bribed staff, masks, behavioral disguise, dead zones, false routes, device discipline, and cleanup.

There is no universal “hack.” Access requires credentials, physical proximity, a compromised insider, a vulnerable device, or time. Deleting one recording does not delete every copy. Systems produce logs. A sophisticated adversary plants false data rather than merely hiding.

Analog systems are useful because they reduce remote attack, but paper can be stolen and people can be pressured. The Administration's typewriters are secure from network intrusion, not betrayal.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Cover Identities and Disguise
Convincing disguise is behavioral. Clothing, posture, speech, documents, familiarity with work, and the confidence to belong matter more than masks.

A cover identity needs:
- A name supported by documents.
- A reason to be present.
- Knowledge appropriate to the role.
- A contact method and history if checked.
- Clothing and tools matching the profession.
- An exit explanation if the operation changes.

Physical alteration through hair, makeup, glasses, facial hair, weight distribution, and posture can reduce recognition. It cannot reliably turn one person into another at close range. High-security access may use multiple identifiers.

A cover is strongest when it asks observers to believe something ordinary.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Gold Coins
Gold Coins are underworld tokens of recognized service, access, and obligation. Their value is ceremonial and contextual rather than a fixed exchange rate.

The common saying is **“one coin for one sanctioned life,”** because a completed ordinary contract often earns at least one coin after brokers and faction shares. In practice, payment varies by risk, status, conditions, and political value. A difficult operation may earn a stack; a wealthy killer may spend several on urgent service.

Coins can purchase or signify:
- One night's standard Continental lodging.
- Basic underworld medical stabilization.
- A weapon introduction or limited equipment package.
- Cleanup of a contained scene.
- Safe transport across a local boundary.
- Archived information of moderate sensitivity.
- Recognition that the bearer participates in the code.

Coins are not legal tender. A shopkeeper outside the network sees precious metal; an underworld professional sees a promise backed by violence and collective recognition.

Counterfeiting coins is an attack on the entire system. Coin presses and dies are guarded as strategic assets.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Markers
A Marker is a blood-sealed record of absolute personal debt. The debtor opens the object, presses a bloodied thumb into one half, and later the holder seals the other half when the debt is fulfilled. The Administration records issuance and satisfaction.

Marker rules:
- The debt must be honored when called.
- The holder cannot demand a service that is literally impossible, but may demand one that is suicidal or morally abhorrent.
- The exact scope is interpreted through precedent, the witnesses, and Table politics.
- The holder may not casually transfer a Marker without registration, though hidden transfers occur.
- A debtor who refuses becomes oathless and may be sanctioned.
- Killing the holder does not erase the record.
- A fulfilled Marker cannot be called again.

Markers are powerful because the underworld collectively enforces them. They are also a weapon of coercion disguised as honor.

At character creation, the player may possess, owe, know about, or secretly have falsified one Marker.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Tickets, Seals, and Other Obligations
**Tickets:** Family or institutional tokens granting passage, one-time assistance, or recognition. A ticket has limits defined by its issuer and may require sacrifice to activate.

**Table Seals:** Credentials carried by Adjudicators, chamberlains, and authorized agents. They command cooperation but create a chain of responsibility.

**Contracts:** Paid obligations recorded through the Administration. Unlike Markers, they can be declined before acceptance unless faction duty says otherwise.

**Vendetta Rights:** Recognized permission for limited retaliation after a serious violation. The scope must be negotiated or violence expands uncontrollably.

**Challenge Tokens:** Used to demand formal arbitration, duel, or trial under an accepted tradition. Not every Table culture recognizes the same form.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Bounties and Excommunicado Status
A bounty is a recorded reward for a target, usually payable upon accepted proof. Open bounties create competitive ecosystems: assassins interfere with one another, brokers sell movement, and ordinary criminals attempt work beyond their ability.

Bounty variables include:
- Alive, dead, or either.
- Geographic validity.
- Expiration.
- Protected methods or locations.
- Escalation schedule.
- Whether collaborators are also sanctioned.

**Excommunicado** strips the target of recognized underworld privileges. Continental services, contract protections, formal payment channels, and ordinary sanctuary are withdrawn. Existing allies face punishment for assistance.

Excommunication does not make every person instantly loyal to the Table. Some help for money, love, revenge, ideology, or opposition. Assistance becomes expensive because it carries institutional risk.

The GM should increase pressure through shrinking options rather than endless identical attackers.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Reputation, Heat, and Standing Procedures
At the end of significant operations, assess three separate changes.

**Reputation increases when:** The player completes difficult work, defeats respected professionals, survives impossible conditions, demonstrates a signature method, or earns public underworld acknowledgment.

**Reputation decreases when:** The player fails visibly, exaggerates, betrays accepted partners, claims another's work, or becomes associated with sloppy civilian casualties.

**Heat increases when:** Evidence remains, violence is public, law enforcement is embarrassed, civilian victims are prominent, devices track movement, or journalists connect patterns.

**Heat decreases when:** Time passes, Cleaners construct plausible alternatives, another suspect is established, the player changes region or identity, or powerful institutions suppress investigation.

**Standing increases when:** Contracts are honored, Markers fulfilled, sanctuary respected, debts paid, and witnesses recognize professional conduct.

**Standing decreases when:** The player violates neutral ground, refuses accepted obligation, kills protected staff, steals from the Administration, counterfeits coins, or embarrasses a sponsor.

High reputation can coexist with low standing. That combination creates legend and crisis.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Faction Loyalty and Defection
Factions provide training, contracts, safehouses, medical access, legal cover, family support, and identity. They also expect obedience.

Loyalty is tested through:
- Work against former friends.
- Silence about internal crimes.
- Financial tribute.
- Marriage or family pressure.
- Requests to train replacements.
- Orders that protect the institution rather than the stated code.

Defection requires a plan for income, documents, medical care, weapons, social ties, and protection from retrieval. Simply announcing independence does not create it.

A faction may accept retirement if the operative pays a price: a final task, surrendered secrets, permanent relocation, a Marker, or loss of status. Some factions never accept it.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Dogs and Working Animals
Trained dogs can track, guard, distract, pursue, detect threats, and fight in close coordination with handlers. They are not remote-controlled weapons.

A handler must consider:
- Trust and bond.
- Commands and line of sight.
- Noise, scent, crowds, and environmental stress.
- Injury, fatigue, and veterinary care.
- Legal and public attention.
- The animal's temperament and refusal.

Dogs may save a handler and may also require the handler to abandon an objective to protect them. Harm to a bonded animal creates emotional and factional consequences.

### POWER_SYSTEM — GM Combat Doctrine
Combat scenes should be built around objectives rather than body counts.

Possible objectives:
- Reach an elevator before lockdown.
- Hold a corridor while an asset is moved.
- Cross a lobby without violating sanctuary.
- Disable a vehicle without killing its passenger.
- Retrieve proof from a body before police arrive.
- Escape with one functional hand.
- Identify which attacker is the actual contract holder.
- Survive until a sanctuary boundary activates.

Use changing terrain: lights fail, sprinklers activate, crowds panic, doors lock, music conceals sound, glass breaks, vehicles block routes, ammunition becomes inaccessible, or a fire forces movement.

Opponents should retreat, communicate, flank, surrender, protect wounded allies, and change tactics. Professionals do not wait in sequence to be defeated.

## 6. ECONOMY

### ECONOMY — Dual Economy: Civilian Money and Underworld Obligation
The underworld uses ordinary money for ordinary needs. Rent, fuel, food, bribery, vehicles, legal businesses, payroll, and international trade remain denominated in local currency. Gold Coins and Markers operate alongside cash as tokens of recognized service and obligation.

A successful assassin therefore manages two kinds of wealth:
- **Liquid wealth:** Cash, accounts, property, businesses, and portable valuables.
- **Standing wealth:** Coins, favors, introductions, Markers owed, sanctuary access, and faction protection.

A person may be rich in money and unable to obtain a Continental room. Another may carry six coins and have no legal cash for a taxi.

### ECONOMY — Contract Payment
Contract payment reflects difficulty, urgency, status, secrecy, and political exposure.

**Routine Local Contract:** One sanctioned target with limited protection. Usually cash plus 1–3 Coins after broker shares.

**Specialist Contract:** Requires infiltration, language, unusual access, or method restrictions. Higher cash, 3–8 Coins, and service support.

**International Contract:** Requires travel identities, logistics, weapons, and local permissions. Substantial cash, 5–15 Coins, expenses, and Continental introductions.

**Political Contract:** Involves a protected family, intelligence service, Table interest, or public figure. Payment may include property, erased debt, a seat's patronage, or a Marker—not merely money.

**Impossible Contract:** The payment is designed to attract legends or create disposable challengers. The true reward may be absolution, freedom, or survival.

Brokers commonly take 10–30 percent depending on who carries risk and provides support. Factions may take a larger share from members in exchange for lifelong infrastructure.

### ECONOMY — Example Coin Costs
Coin costs vary by city and status. These are campaign baselines rather than a universal price list.

- Standard Continental room for one night: **1 Coin**
- Secure room with medical observation: **2–3 Coins**
- Basic concealed handgun package: **1 Coin**
- Mission-specific handgun, ammunition, and holster fitting: **2 Coins**
- Rifle or specialized weapon access: **3–6 Coins**
- Ballistic suit repair: **1–2 Coins**
- New tailored ballistic suit: **4–8 Coins**
- Contained scene cleanup: **2–5 Coins**
- Public or complex cleanup: **8+ Coins plus cash and leverage**
- Emergency local extraction: **2–4 Coins**
- International clean identity: **5–12 Coins**
- Moderate intelligence file: **1–3 Coins**
- Restricted faction file: **Payment negotiated; often a favor**
- Underworld surgery: **3–10 Coins plus recovery debt**
- Courier delivery within a city: **1 Coin**
- Cross-border courier service: **3–7 Coins**
- Temporary safehouse: **1–3 Coins per night**

A service provider may refuse Coins from a person with poor standing or demand more when Table punishment is possible.

### ECONOMY — Markers as Strategic Capital
A Marker cannot be honestly priced. It may be worth a life, a seat, a killing, an escape, or an act that changes history.

Powerful figures accumulate Markers because they convert past rescue into future command. Vulnerable people accept them because immediate survival outweighs future autonomy.

A person holding several Markers is dangerous but also exposed. The debtors may cooperate to kill, discredit, or isolate the holder before the obligations are called.

Marker markets officially do not exist. Unofficially, brokers arrange marriages, inheritances, and transfers around them.

### ECONOMY — Front Businesses and Laundering
Underworld factions require legitimate businesses to move money and people.

Common fronts include:
- Hotels and private clubs.
- Nightclubs, restaurants, and catering firms.
- Shipping, freight, and courier companies.
- Construction and demolition.
- Security and risk consulting.
- Art storage and auction houses.
- Funeral homes and crematoria.
- Medical charities and private clinics.
- Fashion houses and tailoring.
- Vehicle rental and repair.
- Film, theater, and performance companies.
- Religious and cultural foundations.

A front business employs civilians who may know nothing. Destroying it harms ordinary livelihoods and increases heat.

### ECONOMY — Weapons and Supply
Weapons are purchased through legal diversion, theft, corrupt military stocks, private collectors, criminal import, and local manufacture. Crossing borders with a firearm is often more difficult than obtaining one after arrival.

Professionals favor common local ammunition when possible. Exotic weapons make resupply and forensic concealment harder.

A weapon's true cost includes:
- Transport.
- Clean serial history or removed traceability.
- Magazines and tested ammunition.
- Maintenance.
- Training.
- Disposal after use.
- The attention created by possession.

### ECONOMY — Medical Debt
Underworld medicine is costly because it requires secrecy, expertise, facilities, blood products, medication, and staff willing to risk prosecution or execution.

Patients may pay through Coins, cash, future service, or faction guarantee. A surgeon can hold a debt without issuing a formal Marker. These informal medical debts are socially powerful because everyone understands what was given.

A clinic may refuse a patient whose presence threatens all others. A Manager or faction can guarantee protection, but that guarantee becomes political responsibility.

### ECONOMY — Information
Information is often more expensive than weapons because it changes who must be fought.

Information value depends on:
- Freshness.
- Verification.
- Exclusivity.
- Source exposure.
- Political sensitivity.
- Whether possession creates danger.

Low-level information includes routines, addresses, vehicle plates, and public relationships. High-level information includes hidden parentage, Marker ownership, intelligence assets, Table votes, compromised Managers, and true principals behind contracts.

The seller may charge less money in exchange for knowing why the buyer wants it.

### ECONOMY — Retirement
Retirement requires ongoing money and protection. A professional must finance housing, healthcare, documents, security, and silence without visible contract income.

Possible retirement arrangements:
- A legitimate business owned through clean structures.
- A pension paid by a faction in exchange for silence.
- Continental stewardship or training work.
- Rural or foreign relocation under new identity.
- Marriage into protection.
- Permanent service as a broker, handler, or instructor.
- A negotiated release purchased through a final impossible task.

Retirement is fragile when the professional remains famous. Legends are called back because everyone believes only they can solve certain problems.

## 7. EVENTS

### EVENT — The First Continental Charter
Centuries ago, rival criminal houses recognized that constant assassination inside meeting places made negotiation impossible. A group of innkeepers, bankers, smugglers, and killers established the first sanctuary charter. The rule was not born from mercy. It was born from commercial necessity.

The Charter created neutral ground, recognized tokens, and collective punishment for violations. Later Continentals claim direct inheritance from this agreement, though historians dispute how much of the story was invented to legitimize modern Managers.

### EVENT — The Seating of the Twelve
The High Table emerged when twelve dominant criminal powers agreed to recognize shared procedures while retaining internal sovereignty. The exact founding members have changed through extinction, marriage, conquest, and political reclassification.

Every seat claims ancient legitimacy. Several are younger than the institutions they displaced. Records of the transitions are among the Table's most protected documents.

### EVENT — The Night of Twelve Seals
A coordinated attack once targeted Continental properties, Administration offices, and coin vaults across multiple countries. The attackers expected the network to collapse when communications failed. Instead, local Managers followed sealed emergency protocols and continued recognizing sanctuary without central command.

The incident established the Administration's analog redundancy and the doctrine that no single digital or physical center may control the entire ledger.

### EVENT — The Marker War
Two Table houses disputed whether an inherited Marker remained valid after the original holder's death. Each produced witnesses and contradictory records. The conflict expanded into assassinations across three cities before the Harbinger's predecessors imposed a settlement.

The resulting precedent requires formal registration for transfer, but hidden inheritance still occurs. Several old Markers may remain legally ambiguous.

### EVENT — The Ruska Schism
A generation of Ruska Roma graduates challenged the use of family tickets as instruments of permanent control. Some fled into state intelligence services; others were executed or erased from family records. The institution survived by granting limited retirement pathways while quietly preserving its power to recall members.

The names of the principal rebels remain forbidden in some theaters and revered in others.

### EVENT — The Glass House Accords
After a private intelligence corporation exposed three underworld operations to force favorable contracts, the Table threatened total destruction. Negotiation produced the Glass House Accords: corporate intelligence firms may purchase services but cannot claim sanctuary, issue Markers, or independently register global bounties.

The Glass House considers the agreement temporary. The Table considers it proof that corporations understand force.

### EVENT — The Osaka Judgment
A dispute between local loyalty and Table authority led to violence around the Osaka Continental. Different witnesses tell different versions of who violated neutrality first. The official record is sealed.

The incident hardened divisions between Managers who consider hotels autonomous sovereign spaces and Adjudicators who consider them revocable franchises.

### EVENT — The New York Excommunicado Crisis
A legendary assassin's breach of Continental law triggered an escalating bounty, international pursuit, and open conflict involving Managers, the Bowery, Table emissaries, and personal loyalties. The Administration's official account is concise. Underworld retellings vary according to what the speaker wants the story to prove.

The crisis demonstrated that excommunication can increase a target's symbolic power even while stripping practical support.

### EVENT — The Red Archive Leak
A Directorate analyst copied files linking state operations to Continental services, Maison Noire placements, and several Table contracts. The analyst disappeared before releasing the complete archive. Fragments reached journalists, brokers, and the Hollow Archive.

The leak is the main bridge between the espionage world and the assassin world. Every institution wants the missing index because it reveals which documents exist and who was involved.

### EVENT — The Quiet Audit
The current campaign begins during a global review of Continental charters, Marker records, and Table-aligned faction accounts. Adjudicators call it routine. Managers believe the audit is designed to remove independent leadership and consolidate power under a coalition of aggressive seats.

Contracts are being issued to recover records, silence witnesses, test loyalties, and manufacture violations. The player's first assignment is one piece of this conflict, whether their sponsor admits it or not.

### EVENT — The First Contract
The player's faction assigns a target believed to have stolen a ledger fragment from a local broker. The target is scheduled to exchange the fragment during a cultural event, corporate reception, religious service, train journey, or private auction.

The player receives:
- A photograph and civilian identity.
- A location and time window.
- A condition that no civilians be killed.
- One Coin in advance.
- A sealed envelope to be opened only if the target speaks a particular phrase.

Complications:
- The target claims the ledger proves the player's sponsor arranged the death of someone important to the player.
- A rival assassin has orders to kill both the target and the player.
- The exchange location touches a sanctuary boundary.
- The sealed envelope contains a dormant Marker bearing the player's blood or family seal.

This event should launch the campaign without requiring immediate rebellion. The player may complete the contract, protect the target, steal the evidence, negotiate with the rival, or attempt to satisfy the letter of the order while subverting its purpose.

### EVENT — The Ledger Without a Name
A new bounty appears through legitimate Administration channels, but the target field is blank. The reward increases every twelve hours. Professionals begin killing anyone rumored to fit the hidden criteria.

The event may be a clerical attack, a Table loyalty test, an automated dead-man instruction, or an attempt to destabilize a city. The player must decide whether to pursue the reward, protect likely targets, or discover who has authority to publish a contract without a name.

### EVENT — The Last Marker
Rumor claims a Marker exists bearing the blood of a current Table member from before they inherited a seat. If genuine, an unknown holder could command an act capable of changing the council.

Multiple factions search for the Marker while publicly denying it exists. Some want to use it. Others want it destroyed. The person who physically carries it may not know what it is worth.

### EVENT — The Managers’ Conclave
Continental Managers are summoned to a rare private meeting to discuss the Quiet Audit. Each arrives with minimal security under a temporary sanctuary declaration. One Manager fails to appear, and their hotel loses communication hours later.

The player may be hired as a courier, external guard, investigator, or deniable agent. The real conflict concerns whether sanctuary belongs to the Table or whether the Table depends on sanctuary.
