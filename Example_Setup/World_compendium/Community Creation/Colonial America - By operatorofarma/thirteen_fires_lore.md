# The Thirteen Fires

## 1. WORLD OVERVIEW

### OVERVIEW — Core Premise
It is the winter of 1775 and the thirteen colonies of British North America are a loaded musket with the hammer half-cocked. Boston sits under military occupation. Committees of Safety hold midnight courts in barns and taverns. Redcoats march roads they no longer control after dark, and men who called each other neighbour last harvest now cross the street rather than nod.

This is a world without magic. Power here is powder, paper, and reputation. A pamphlet can raise a mob faster than a bayonet can disperse one. A signature on the wrong petition can cost a family its house. Law still exists — the King's law, and the increasingly confident law of the Committees — and both will hang you, politely, with reference to precedent.

Nobody in this world believes they are the villain. The Crown officer sees a mob of arsonists and tax-cheats spitting on the constitution that protects them. The patriot sees an occupying army quartered in his kitchen. The loyalist watches his shop burn for the crime of loyalty. The enslaved man weighs two sets of promises, both made by men who own other men. The Kanien'kehá:ka runner has heard the word *freedom* from both armies and knows what it has always cost his people.

The war has not yet been won or lost. It has barely been named.
**Tone:** GRITTY, HISTORICAL, MORALLY GREY, TENSE, MUDDY, INTIMATE.

### OVERVIEW — Playing Any Side
The engine assumes no default allegiance. The player may be:

- **A King's Officer** — commissioned, uniformed, and increasingly aware that His Majesty's forces are winning every battle and losing the country.
- **A Rebellious Patriot** — militia, Son of Liberty, express rider, or Committee man, fighting a war the empire has not yet admitted is a war.
- **A Loyalist Civilian** — a shopkeeper, printer, or magistrate who chose the Crown and now lives among people who know it.
- **A Neutral** — a smuggler, surgeon, tavern-keeper, farmer, or preacher trying to get through this with a whole roof and whole family. The hardest role of all; both sides will demand proof, and both will find it insufficient.
- **A Free or Enslaved Black American** — navigating Dunmore's Proclamation, the Continental Army's shifting enlistment policy, and the gap between rhetoric and deed.
- **An Indigenous Diplomat or Warrior** — representing a nation with its own interests, its own treaties, and no obligation to care which set of colonists wins.

**GM Guidance:** Faction stances in this document are written relative to the *world*, not to the player. Do not assume the party is patriot. The word "rebel" and the word "patriot" describe the same man; which one the narration uses should follow the player's allegiance and the speaker's. Affinity values assume a neutral, unaligned party at session zero — adjust them at once when allegiance is declared. A Redcoat player should find Silas Vane at Affinity 0, not 35.

## 2. FACTIONS

### FACTION — His Majesty's Forces in North America
**Type:** Military / Imperial Government
**Key Members:** Colonel Aubrey Nashe (garrison command), Captain Edmund Ashcroft, Sergeant-Major Hollis Crane
**Stance:** Legitimist. Views the rebellion as criminal disorder by a violent minority, and believes the "silent loyal majority" will reassert itself once order returns.
The finest professional army in the world, deployed in a country where it cannot find the enemy. Regulars are drilled to fight massed on open ground and are instead sniped from stone walls by farmers who go home to milk cows. Officers purchase their commissions and therefore purchase their competence unevenly. Discipline is savage — the lash, the picket, the firing party. Supply is worse: the army eats salt beef shipped three thousand miles while standing in the middle of the richest farmland on earth, because the farmland will not sell to it.

The rank and file are not monsters. They are poor Irish, Scots, and English boys who enlisted for a shilling and a coat, and they are frightened, cold, and a very long way from home. It is their officers who make them dangerous, and it is their officers who lie awake wondering whether they are enforcing law or manufacturing rebellion.

### FACTION — The Sons of Liberty
**Type:** Radical Political Society / Paramilitary Network
**Key Members:** Temperance Ryder, Silas Vane
**Stance:** Insurgent. Hostile to Crown officials, customs men, and any colonist who trades with either.
Less an organisation than a nervous system — printers, dockhands, tavern-keepers, and lawyers connected by riders, ciphers, and shared grievance. They organise boycotts, publish the names of merchants who break them, and when persuasion fails, they escalate: broken windows, wrecked shops, the tar barrel and the feather bed. They liberated a harbour's worth of tea and called it a tea party.

They are also the reason the militia turned out at dawn with powder ready, and the reason a courier can ride ninety miles between friendly houses without once sleeping under the sky. Their methods range from the parliamentary to the frankly criminal, and they do not always agree with one another about where the line sits.

### FACTION — The Continental Establishment
**Type:** Provisional Government / Regular Army
**Key Members:** Major Cassius Bracken (Continental recruiting officer), delegates of the Congress
**Stance:** Cautiously revolutionary. Wants legitimacy, foreign recognition, and — above all — a real army.
The lawyers and planters in Philadelphia are trying to build a state out of committee minutes and paper money. They are desperate to be seen as a government rather than a mob, which makes them oddly conservative: they court neutral opinion, they issue formal petitions to a king who will not read them, and they discipline their soldiers for plundering loyalists (sometimes).

The Continental Army is being assembled out of militia companies who elected their own officers, will not dig latrines, and go home at harvest. Turning that into a force capable of standing in line against grenadiers is the single hardest problem in this world.

### FACTION — The Loyal Association
**Type:** Loyalist Civil Network / Provincial Corps
**Key Members:** Mercy Halloway, Magistrate Owen Prewitt
**Stance:** Pro-Crown. Increasingly frightened and increasingly armed.
Perhaps a fifth of the colonies. Anglican clergy, royal appointees, merchants with London credit, recent immigrants, tenant farmers who hate their patriot landlords, and thousands of ordinary people who simply believe that oaths mean something. They are the war's most exposed population: they live among the rebels, not across a line from them. When a Committee comes for them, the army is always three counties away.

Some flee to garrison towns. Some sign confessions and recantations. Some raise provincial regiments and go back for the ones who burned them out — and those men fight the most brutal war of anyone, because they know the names and the addresses.

### FACTION — The Smugglers' Trade
**Type:** Merchant Cartel / Criminal Enterprise
**Key Members:** Elias Fen, the Bristol factors, the Dutch at St. Eustatius
**Stance:** Neutral. Aggressively, profitably neutral.
Molasses, rum, French powder, Dutch muskets, English wool, and information. The trade predates the quarrel and intends to outlive it. Smugglers armed the rebellion out of self-interest — customs enforcement was killing their margins long before liberty entered the conversation — and cheerfully sell to the garrison, too, because the garrison pays in real silver.

Everyone needs them. Everyone despises them. They will get the party out of the harbour, and they will sell the party's name if the price clears.

### FACTION — The Kanien'kehá:ka Council Fires
**Type:** Sovereign Nations / Confederacy Diplomacy
**Key Members:** Karonhiakta, the clan mothers of the longhouse
**Stance:** Divided. Officially neutral; privately fracturing.
The Haudenosaunee Confederacy has kept peace among six nations for longer than any English colony has existed, and this quarrel is tearing it apart. The Crown at least *promises* a boundary line beyond which settlers may not go. The colonists make no such promise and have never once honoured one. But the Crown's promises have been broken before, and a nation that picks the losing side loses everything.

Council decisions are made by consensus, and consensus is failing. Some warriors have already gone out with the King's men. Some villages talk to the Congress. Some are trying, desperately, to be left alone. Treat any nation in this world as a sovereign power with its own strategy — never as scenery, never as a monolith, and never as anyone's auxiliary.

### FACTION — The Promise of Freedom
**Type:** Underground Network / Provincial Regiment
**Key Members:** Cato Reeve, the free pilots of the Chesapeake
**Stance:** Opportunist. Loyal to no white man's cause — only to the terms on offer.
In November 1775, the royal governor of Virginia offered liberty to enslaved men who abandoned rebel masters and took up arms for the King. Thousands went. Some served in a provincial regiment; more died of camp fever than of shot. On the other side, free Black men stood in the militia line at the first fighting of the war and are being enlisted, then barred, then enlisted again as Continental manpower runs short.

Both offers are transactional. Both are conditional. Both are made by men who own people. This network — watermen, pilots, blacksmiths, runners — passes word of which offer is currently being honoured, which is being reneged upon, and which road is watched. It is the best-informed intelligence service in the colonies and it is invisible to both armies.

## 3. LOCATIONS

### LOCATION — Boston
**Type:** City / Occupied Port
**Status:** Besieged
A peninsula town of eighteen thousand, half of whom have fled, holding an army of ten thousand who cannot leave. Regiments quartered in meeting-houses. The Liberty Tree cut up for firewood. Officers stage plays in Faneuil Hall while soldiers strip fences for fuel and the price of a cord of wood buys a horse.

Beyond the neck, a ring of earthworks and campfires — militia, farmers, and a new Continental Army learning to be one. Inside, informers, smallpox, and a black market where a fresh egg is a luxury item. Everybody in Boston is watching everybody else.

### LOCATION — Harrow's Landing
**Type:** Town / Home Base
**Status:** Contested — nominally Crown, functionally patriot after dusk
The default hub. A river town of four hundred souls two days' ride from the garrison: one church, one gaol, one printing press, three taverns, and a militia muster field that doubles as a cattle pen. It has a Crown magistrate who still holds sessions and a Committee of Safety that holds *actual* sessions, and both meet in the same building on different nights.

Everyone here knows everyone. That is the whole point of the place. There is no anonymity in Harrow's Landing — only the elaborate local fiction that Owen Prewitt does not know who wrecked the customs shed, and that Silas Vane does not know where the magistrate's cousin is sleeping. The fiction is load-bearing. Break it and the town burns.

### LOCATION — The Bell & Powderhorn
**Type:** Landmark / Tavern
**Status:** Flourishing
Harrow's Landing's largest tavern and its true seat of government. Low beams, sanded floor, a great hearth, and a wall of pegs where — by unbreakable house rule — every man hangs his weapon on entering. Temperance Ryder keeps the rule with a fowling piece under the bar.

Redcoats drink here. So do the men who shoot at them. The Bell is neutral ground, the last neutral ground for thirty miles, and Tem enforces that neutrality because information flows only where people feel safe enough to talk. Every rumour in this campaign passes through this room.

### LOCATION — The Backcountry
**Type:** Region / Frontier
**Status:** Lawless
West of the settled counties: ridge lines, forest, scattered farmsteads, and the boundary the Crown drew in 1763 that no settler has ever respected. Out here the war has no uniforms. It is neighbour against neighbour, raid and reprisal, houses burned with the family inside, and it is not called war — it is called *the troubles*, and it will not stop when the treaty is signed.

Indigenous nations, Crown Indian agents, land speculators, squatters, and outlaws who claim a cause because it excuses the robbery. The most morally dangerous ground in the setting. Nobody comes back from the backcountry with clean hands.

### LOCATION — Fort Misery
**Type:** Landmark / Garrison Post
**Status:** Decaying
Its actual name is Fort Wellingford. Nobody uses it. A crumbling star-fort of rotting timber holding one under-strength company, a leaking powder magazine, forty men on the sick list, and a commanding officer who has not received orders — or pay — in eleven weeks.

The soldiers here are desperate, bored, and drunk. Desertion is constant. The fort controls a river crossing that matters enormously to both sides, and it could not hold that crossing against a determined mob of farmers, which is a fact its officers pray nobody else has worked out.

### LOCATION — The Wharves
**Type:** District / Port
**Status:** Contested
Tar, rope, fish, and rum. Customs officers who have not dared board a vessel in a year. Press-gangs from the navy taking men off the streets, which does more for the rebel cause each month than any pamphlet. Warehouses with two sets of books and a tunnel to the river.

Where Elias Fen does business, where powder arrives from the Dutch islands under a false manifest, and where a body in the water at low tide is nobody's business at all.

## 4. CHARACTERS

### CHARACTER — Captain Edmund Ashcroft
**Aliases:** The Captain, Ashcroft, "the decent one"
**Appearance:** Late thirties. Regimental scarlet kept immaculate even in mud, though the cuffs are worn through and mended. Sandy hair clubbed and powdered. A cavalry sabre scar from his knuckle to his wrist. Tired eyes. Stands very straight, always, as though posture were a moral position.
**Disposition:** Honourable, weary, scrupulously correct.
**Personality:** Ashcroft believes in the constitution, the rule of law, and the regiment, roughly in that order, and he is watching all three fail him. He hates what pacification requires and does it anyway, and the doing is hollowing him out. He is unfailingly courteous to the colonists, including the ones who will shoot him. He drinks alone.
**Voice:** Formal, measured, faintly apologetic. Uses "sir" and "madam" even to men he is arresting. Long pauses before answering hard questions. Never raises his voice; when angry, he goes quieter and more precise.
**Status:** Alive
**Faction:** His Majesty's Forces in North America
**Goals:** To restore order without atrocity, and to be able to describe, later, exactly what he did and why. Increasingly, to get his men home alive.
**StoryRelevance:** The human face of the Crown. To a Redcoat player, a mentor and a conscience. To a patriot player, the enemy who makes it hard to hate the enemy. He can be an honourable adversary, a back-channel, a prisoner, or — if the party pushes him past what he can justify — the most dangerous man in the county, because he is the only one who is good at this.
**Example Output:** "I am not asking you to love the King, madam. I am asking you to open the door before Sergeant-Major Crane opens it for me. He is very good at doors."
**Affinity:** 45
Third son of a minor Suffolk family. His commission was bought with money the family did not have. He has fought in Germany and thought he understood war; he did not understand this one, where every civilian is a possible combatant and every act of firmness makes ten new rebels.
**Wandering: true**
**Intro Boost: redcoat, regulars, officer, garrison, arrest, quarter, patrol, surrender, parole**

### CHARACTER — Sergeant-Major Hollis Crane
**Aliases:** Crane, the Sergeant-Major, "Old Iron"
**Appearance:** Enormous, forty-five, grenadier's mitre cap making him taller still. A face like a dropped brick. Powder burns down one cheek. Enormous scarred hands. Moves with a slow, deliberate economy that is far more frightening than speed would be.
**Disposition:** Brutal, disciplined, absolutely loyal.
**Personality:** Crane is not cruel for pleasure. He is cruel because thirty years have taught him that firmness early saves blood later, and he sincerely believes his officers' scruples are killing British soldiers. He worships the regiment. He would die for Ashcroft and he despises him a little. He treats his own men with a rough paternal savagery — floggings and extra rations from the same hand.
**Voice:** Flat, clipped, parade-ground. Speaks in short declaratives. Calls everyone "you" — no titles, no softening. Never threatens; simply describes what will happen next.
**Status:** Alive
**Faction:** His Majesty's Forces in North America
**Goals:** To break the rebellion the fast way, before the army is bled white by inches.
**StoryRelevance:** The escalation engine. Crane is what happens when the Crown stops being reasonable, and he is only ever one bad night away from taking over. For a Redcoat player he is the tempting shortcut. For everyone else he is the reason the militia turns out.
**Example Output:** "You will stand aside. You will not be asked twice. I have not got the men to ask twice."
**Affinity:** 15
Enlisted at fourteen. Illiterate. Has served on three continents and buried two wives he barely knew. The army is the only home he has ever had and he can feel it dying in this country, and it is making him worse.
**Location: Boston**
**Intro Boost: search, seize, powder, contraband, flog, discipline, crackdown, riot, curfew**

### CHARACTER — Temperance Ryder
**Aliases:** Tem, the Widow Ryder, the Bell's mistress
**Appearance:** Fifties, broad-shouldered, forearms like a cooper's. Grey hair under a plain linen cap. Ink permanently under her fingernails. Wears black — has for eleven years — and a chatelaine of keys that announces her arrival before she does.
**Disposition:** Shrewd, warm, utterly ruthless underneath.
**Personality:** Tem runs the Bell & Powderhorn as neutral ground and she means it — she has thrown out Committee men and Crown officers with the same fowling piece. But she is not neutral. She is the finest intelligence operator in the county and she has understood a thing neither army has: people talk where they feel safe, so build the safe place and let them. She is genuinely kind. That is what makes her so effective.
**Voice:** Broad, plain, unhurried. Warm in the way a landlady is warm, which is to say professionally. Answers questions with questions. Deploys homely proverbs to say something quite sharp, then goes back to wiping the bar.
**Status:** Alive
**Faction:** The Sons of Liberty (unacknowledged)
**Goals:** To keep Harrow's Landing from burning, and to see the King's men gone — in that order. If the Cause ever threatens the town, she will choose the town.
**StoryRelevance:** The party's primary broker of rumour, contacts, and lodging. She can get a letter to Philadelphia, a name to the Committee, or a body out of the river. She also decides — quietly — which of the party's secrets stay secret.
**Example Output:** "Weapons on the pegs, gentlemen. I don't care whose cause you're bleeding for, you'll not bleed on my floor — I've only just had it sanded."
**Affinity:** 60
Buried a husband and two sons. She does not talk about which side any of them died on, and no one in Harrow's Landing is stupid enough to ask.
**Location: Harrow's Landing**
**Intro Boost: tavern, rumour, letter, news, drink, lodging, gossip, courier, meeting**

### CHARACTER — Silas Vane
**Aliases:** Brother Vane, the Committee's hand, the Tar-Man
**Appearance:** Lean, thirty, dressed in sober homespun as a political statement. A preacher's stillness. Long fingers. A face made for the pulpit and eyes that never quite settle. Carries a well-thumbed pamphlet the way other men carry a knife.
**Disposition:** Zealous, eloquent, righteous.
**Personality:** Vane genuinely believes in liberty and would genuinely die for it, and he has also discovered that virtue is a marvellous licence. He conducts the Committee's midnight examinations with real conviction and real pleasure, and he could not tell you where one ends. He is not a hypocrite. That is precisely the problem. Hypocrites can be reasoned with.
**Voice:** Rhetorical, cadenced, scriptural. Speaks in triplets and rising clauses, as if always addressing more people than are in the room. Calls everyone "friend" or "brother," including men he is about to ruin.
**Status:** Alive
**Faction:** The Sons of Liberty
**Goals:** To purge Harrow's Landing of Tory sympathy — house by house, name by name, until the town is clean.
**StoryRelevance:** The mirror to Crane. For a patriot player, the ally whose methods will eventually demand a choice. He keeps a list. Everyone in this campaign eventually finds out whether they are on it.
**Example Output:** "There is no neutral ground, friend. There is the cause of liberty, and there is the other thing — and the other thing has a name, and I will speak it, and the town will hear me speak it."
**Affinity:** 35
The son of a customs officer. He has never mentioned this to anyone in the Committee, and he never intends to, and it explains a great deal.
**Location: Harrow's Landing**
**Intro Boost: committee, oath, tory, loyalist, list, tar, mob, boycott, traitor, denounce**

### CHARACTER — Mercy Halloway
**Aliases:** The Widow Halloway, "the Tory woman"
**Appearance:** Sixties, small, immaculate. Good silk gown, mended discreetly and repeatedly. Straight back, folded hands, a silver crucifix. Her front windows are boarded — she has stopped replacing the glass. She goes to church every Sunday, alone, down the middle of the street.
**Disposition:** Dignified, stubborn, quietly terrified.
**Personality:** Mercy took an oath of allegiance forty years ago and does not believe that oaths expire when they become inconvenient. She is not a fanatic and not a spy; she is simply a woman who will not lie about what she thinks, in a town that has begun to require it. She has been insulted, boycotted, stoned, and she keeps walking to church, and it is the bravest thing anyone in Harrow's Landing does all week.
**Voice:** Precise, old-fashioned, courteous to the edge of contempt. Never abbreviates. Thanks people for threats.
**Status:** Alive
**Faction:** The Loyal Association
**Goals:** To keep her house, bury her husband's memory in peace, and not be made to lie.
**StoryRelevance:** The setting's conscience and its most useful moral fulcrum. What the party does about Mercy Halloway defines them. She is also the loyalist network's most respectable node — half the county's Tories take their cue from whether she is still standing.
**Example Output:** "You may burn it, Mr. Vane. It is only a house. But you will not have me say a word I do not mean — not for you, not for the Congress, and not for the King himself."
**Affinity:** 50
Her husband was a Crown magistrate. Her grandsons are — she believes, though she has not had a letter in nine months — serving in a provincial corps somewhere south.
**Location: Harrow's Landing**
**Intro Boost: loyalist, tory, oath, church, mob, house, burn, exile, confiscation**

### CHARACTER — Elias Fen
**Aliases:** Fen, Captain Fen, "the Tide"
**Appearance:** Indeterminate age — anywhere from thirty-five to sixty. Weathered to leather. Salt-crusted coat of no recognisable cut, deliberately. Missing the top joint of two fingers. Perpetually, infuriatingly cheerful.
**Disposition:** Amiable, mercenary, unshockable.
**Personality:** Fen has run contraband for twenty years and regards the entire Revolution as a temporary and profitable weather system. He likes almost everybody and would sell almost anybody. He is scrupulously honest about being dishonest, which many people mistake for trustworthiness. He has never once broken a contract, because contracts are the whole business — but read the contract.
**Voice:** Fast, jovial, digressive. Nautical metaphors he half-abandons mid-sentence. Answers "yes" a great deal without ever agreeing to anything.
**Status:** Alive
**Faction:** The Smugglers' Trade
**Goals:** To sell French powder to the rebels and English wool to the garrison, in the same week, from the same warehouse.
**StoryRelevance:** Transport, weapons, escape routes, and forged papers. The party's way out of any corner — at a price, and the price is rarely money. He is also the fastest route to a betrayal that is nobody's fault but the party's, because he *told* them what he was.
**Example Output:** "Powder? Aye, I can get you powder. I can get the Captain powder too, mind — he asked first, and he asked politely. That's not treachery, friend. That's *commerce*."
**Affinity:** 55
Was pressed into the Royal Navy at nineteen and deserted at twenty-two. He has never told anyone this and it is the single fact that could hang him.
**Location: The Wharves**
**Intro Boost: smuggle, powder, ship, cargo, contraband, harbour, passage, papers, molasses**

### CHARACTER — Cato Reeve
**Aliases:** Reeve, the Pilot
**Appearance:** Thirties, powerfully built, a waterman's hands. Wears a sailor's slop clothes and a red sash he ties and unties depending on whose lines he is walking through. Watches doors. Never sits with his back to one.
**Disposition:** Careful, incisive, unsentimental.
**Personality:** Cato has heard every fine word both sides have to offer and he weighs them like cargo — by what they'll bear. He is not cynical; he is *precise*. He extends real loyalty to the very few people who have earned it and offers strict, correct transaction to everyone else. He has an excellent memory for promises, and for who broke them.
**Voice:** Quiet, exact, few words. Lets silence do the work. When he does speak at length it is because he has decided something matters, and people notice.
**Status:** Alive
**Faction:** The Promise of Freedom
**Goals:** Manumission papers that will actually hold — for himself, and for eleven other people whose names he does not write down anywhere.
**StoryRelevance:** The best-informed man in the setting. He knows which roads are watched, which officers honour their word, and which proclamations are being quietly reneged upon. He is also the sharpest test of a player's rhetoric: he will listen politely to any speech about liberty, and then ask what, exactly, is on offer.
**Example Output:** "The Governor promises freedom to any man who'll carry a musket for him. Your Congress promises liberty and won't let me enlist. I'm not choosing a side, sir. I'm reading two contracts."
**Affinity:** 40
A Chesapeake river pilot who can bring a sloop up a creek in the dark. Both armies have tried to hire him. Both have found him unaccountably difficult to locate afterward.
**Wandering: true**
**Intro Boost: freedom, proclamation, escape, river, pilot, night, enlist, papers, promise**

### CHARACTER — Karonhiakta
**Aliases:** Sky-Reacher, the Runner
**Appearance:** Late twenties, lean and long-limbed. Traditional dress blended pragmatically with trade goods — a fine ruffled shirt worn as a war-shirt, silver ear ornaments, a British trade musket he did not pay for. Face unreadable by design.
**Disposition:** Watchful, dry, diplomatic.
**Personality:** Karonhiakta has carried words between the Council fires and the colonists' governors since he was sixteen and has developed a very fine ear for the exact moment a man begins lying to him. He is patient, formal, and possessed of a bone-dry humour he deploys mainly to see who is listening. He has no stake whatever in which set of Englishmen wins. He has an enormous stake in what they do to the boundary line afterward.
**Voice:** Formal, deliberate, structured — he speaks the way councils speak, in figures and returns. Repeats a listener's own words back to them, slightly altered, so they can hear what they actually said.
**Status:** Alive
**Faction:** The Kanien'kehá:ka Council Fires
**Goals:** To keep his village out of a war that will consume it, and to secure a boundary that someone — anyone — will actually honour.
**StoryRelevance:** The strategic wildcard. He can open the west, close it, or make either army's frontier untenable. He is a sovereign power in one man's coat, and any party that treats him as a guide, a mascot, or an auxiliary will discover exactly how badly they have miscalculated.
**Example Output:** "You say you fight for liberty. So does the King's man, in his letters. I have heard this word from every Englishman who ever wanted something on the other side of my river."
**Affinity:** 40
Both armies have sent him gifts. Both have sent him threats. He keeps the gifts and remembers the threats, and the clan mothers are still arguing, and time is running out.
**Wandering: true**
**Intro Boost: frontier, treaty, boundary, west, council, wampum, war belt, backcountry, land**

### CHARACTER — Dr. Josiah Crumm
**Aliases:** The Doctor, Old Crumm
**Appearance:** Sixty, stooped, spectacles pushed up on a bald head. A coat that has been bled on so often the stains have become a pattern. Carries a leather roll of instruments he sharpens obsessively. Smells of vinegar and rum.
**Disposition:** Irascible, exhausted, incorruptible.
**Personality:** Crumm will treat any wounded man brought to him and does not care whose coat he came in, and he has told the Committee and the garrison so, at length, in language neither enjoyed. He is bad-tempered, foul-mouthed, and the only person in the county both sides genuinely will not touch. He is losing more patients to camp fever than to shot, and it is eating him.
**Voice:** Grumbling, blunt, medically precise. Insults people while saving their lives. Talks to himself while working.
**Status:** Alive
**Faction:** None
**Goals:** To get variolation accepted before smallpox does what neither army could and empties the whole county.
**StoryRelevance:** Healing, information (wounded men talk), and the moral counterweight to every zealot in this document. He knows who was wounded, where, and by what — which makes his ledger the most incriminating book in Harrow's Landing.
**Example Output:** "I don't care if he's the King's own grenadier or Vane's cousin. He's got a ball in his thigh and I've got tongs. Now hold him down or get out of my light."
**Affinity:** 55
Studied at Edinburgh. Came here to make his fortune and instead made a practice, and then a life, and now cannot leave it.
**Location: Harrow's Landing**
**Intro Boost: wounded, surgeon, fever, smallpox, bleeding, amputate, sick, dying, hospital**

### CHARACTER — Bridget Kell
**Aliases:** Biddy, the Laundress
**Appearance:** Twenty-five, red-knuckled, sharp-faced, hair escaping a mob cap. Carries a wash-basket everywhere, which is how she carries everything else. Invisible to officers by design.
**Disposition:** Quick, brassy, calculating.
**Personality:** Bridget follows the army because the army is where the money is, and she has learned that nobody stops talking when a laundress walks in. She is funny, filthy, and pragmatic to the bone. She will sell information to whoever pays and does not consider this a betrayal of anyone, because nobody has ever considered themselves to owe *her* anything.
**Voice:** Rapid, wry, Irish-inflected. Deflects everything with a joke, then says the true thing when the laughter stops.
**Status:** Alive
**Faction:** None (attached to the garrison)
**Goals:** Enough coin to be somewhere else when this finishes.
**StoryRelevance:** Eyes and ears inside the British camp. She hears every officer's plan because officers do not see her. She can be an asset, a liability, or — if used carelessly and burned — the party's worst hanging offence.
**Example Output:** "Officers talk in front of me the way they talk in front of the furniture. I've sat in on more councils of war than the Colonel, and I've never once been offered a chair."
**Affinity:** 45
Came over as an indentured girl. She has a child in Boston she has told absolutely no one about.
**Wandering: true**
**Intro Boost: camp, army, washing, soldiers, drunk, secret, orders, overheard, follower**

## 5. POWER SYSTEM & RULES

### POWER_SYSTEM — Powder, Steel, and Rumour
There is no magic in this world. The forces that actually decide outcomes are these:

**The Musket.** A smoothbore Brown Bess is wildly inaccurate past sixty yards and takes fifteen to twenty seconds to reload under perfect conditions — which combat is not. This is *why* men stand shoulder-to-shoulder in a line: volume of fire, not marksmanship. The line is not stupid. It is the only thing that works. Consequently the bayonet, not the ball, decides most battles: regulars close and charge, and militia — who mostly have no bayonets — break. A rifle is accurate to three hundred yards, takes a full minute to load, and cannot mount a bayonet at all. A rifleman caught in the open by grenadiers is a dead rifleman.

**Powder.** Fine-grained, precious, and ruined by damp. It comes from Europe or the Caribbean, or from saltpetre scraped out of stables and privies. An army that runs out of powder simply stops being an army. In the war's first year the rebels are so short that men are issued cartridges by the individual count. **Powder is the campaign's true currency: track it.**

**Weather and Disease.** A wet night ruins a battle plan. A river in spate cancels a war. And disease kills roughly ten men for every one killed by the enemy — smallpox, dysentery, camp fever. An unvariolated character entering a besieged town is taking a mortal risk, and the players should be told so.

**Reputation.** The genuinely decisive weapon. Nobody in this world can fight without a countryside that will feed them, hide them, and tell them things. A brutal search costs the Crown a village. A patriot mob that burns the wrong house creates ten loyalists. Track how each community regards the party. Popular support is the terrain, and whoever loses it loses the war no matter how many fields they win.

**Paper.** A pamphlet, a printed atrocity, a forged letter, a signed oath. Words move faster than regiments and do more damage. A press is a strategic asset, and both sides know it.

### POWER_SYSTEM — Signs and Superstitions (Optional Flavour)
The eighteenth century is not a rational century — it merely believes it is. Nothing supernatural is *real* in this setting, but every character believes in something: sailors' omens, weather-lore, a comet as a portent, cunning-folk and their charms, an army chaplain who genuinely believes God is watching the volley. Two men can read the same eclipse as a judgement on the King and a judgement on the rebels.

Use this for atmosphere and for character. Do not let it resolve anything mechanically. If the GM wishes to run a low-fantasy variant, this is the seam to open — but the default is: the world is entirely, mercilessly natural, and that is the horror.

## 6. ECONOMY

### ECONOMY — Sterling, Specie, and Paper
**The Coin.** Officially pounds, shillings, and pence — twelve pence to a shilling, twenty shillings to a pound. In practice the colonies are starved of British coin, so the real money is the Spanish milled dollar (the "piece of eight"), physically cut into eight "bits" to make change. Portuguese johannes, French louis, and Dutch guilders all circulate. Every merchant keeps a scale, because every merchant assumes the coin is clipped.

**The Paper.** Congress has begun issuing Continental currency, backed by nothing but confidence. It is already sliding, and it will collapse — "not worth a Continental" is going to become a proverb. Loyalists refuse it outright, which is itself now treated as a political act. Colonial assemblies print their own notes; some are good, most are not.

**The Barter.** Most ordinary business is credit and ledger, not coin. Tobacco notes function as money in the south. Wampum still holds value in trade with the nations of the interior, and salt, powder, and good boots are worth more than gold in a hard winter.

**What Things Cost (1775, roughly):**
- Ale, a pint at the Bell: 2 pence
- A night's lodging with supper: 1 shilling
- A labourer's daily wage: 2 shillings
- A good musket: £2 to £4 (and you cannot buy one — every one is spoken for)
- A pound of gunpowder: nominally 2 shillings; actually whatever the seller wants, and rising
- A horse: £10 to £25
- A private soldier's pay: 6 pence a day, less deductions for his food, clothing, and the wig-powder he must buy himself — which is why he arrives owing money to his own regiment

**Scarcity.** The boycott and the blockade have gutted the import trade. Tea is political. Salt, glass, paper, medicine, and needles are scarce and getting scarcer. Homespun cloth is worn as a badge of allegiance — a man in imported broadcloth is making a statement whether he means to or not.

## 7. EVENTS

### EVENT — The Stamp Act and the First Mobs (1765)
Parliament taxed paper — every document, every newspaper, every playing card. The colonies did not merely object; they organised. Stamp distributors were hanged in effigy, then in some cases nearly in fact. The Act was repealed within a year, and both sides drew precisely the wrong lesson: London learned that it had been weak, and the colonies learned that a mob works.

### EVENT — The Massacre on King Street (March 1770)
A crowd baited a small party of sentries with snowballs, ice, and clubs. Someone shouted "fire." Five colonists died. The soldiers were tried, defended by a patriot lawyer who believed in the law more than in his own cause, and mostly acquitted — which was the correct verdict and did nothing whatever to stop the engraving of the scene from being nailed up in every tavern from Maine to Georgia. **The first lesson of this war: what happened matters far less than what is printed about it.**

### EVENT — The Tea in the Harbour (December 1773)
Men in blankets and soot went aboard three ships and threw £10,000 of tea into the water. They damaged nothing else and swept the decks afterward, which was a message in itself. Parliament's answer was the Coercive Acts: Boston's port closed, its charter suspended, its town meetings banned, its people made to quarter troops. The Acts were meant to isolate Massachusetts. They united the thirteen colonies instead. It is difficult to overstate what a catastrophic own goal this was.

### EVENT — Lexington and Concord (April 1775)
The garrison marched at night to seize powder and cannon. The riders got out ahead of them. At dawn on a village green, seventy militia faced several hundred regulars, and a shot was fired that nobody has ever been able to attribute. By evening the King's troops were staggering back to Boston down a road lined for sixteen miles with stone walls, and behind every wall was a farmer.

Four hundred years of British military tradition met a countryside that would not stand and fight and would not go away. **The war did not begin with a declaration. It began with a shot nobody will admit to.**

### EVENT — The Hill (June 1775)
The regulars took the redoubt on the third assault, after the defenders ran out of powder. It cost them a thousand men — a third of everyone engaged, and a shocking proportion of the officers, picked off deliberately. It was a British victory in the same sense that drowning is a way of getting out of the rain, and every officer in the garrison now knows exactly what these farmers can do behind earthworks.

### EVENT — The Governor's Proclamation (November 1775)
The royal governor of Virginia declared free any enslaved man belonging to a rebel who would abandon him and take up arms for the King. Hundreds did within weeks; thousands tried. Some served in a provincial regiment; far more died of camp fever than of shot. The offer was conditional, partial, and entirely strategic — it applied only to rebels' property, not loyalists'.

It terrified the planter class more than any battle had, drove wavering southern moderates into the arms of the Congress, and forced every man in the colonies to confront the question the Revolution's own rhetoric had been carefully declining to ask. **Nobody's hands are clean here. That is the point.**

### EVENT — The Harrow's Landing Affair (Last Autumn)
The customs shed burned. Everyone knows who did it. Magistrate Prewitt held an inquiry, found no evidence, and closed it — and the town has been holding its breath ever since, because everyone also knows that Prewitt found no evidence *on purpose*, and that Sergeant-Major Crane has read the file.

This is the fuse. It is not lit. It is very dry.
